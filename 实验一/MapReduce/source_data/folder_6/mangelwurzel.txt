 The '''mangelwurzel''' (from [[:de:Futterrübe|German]]) is also called 'mangel wurzel', 'mangold wurzel', 'mangold', < ref name= " Wright " > Wright, Clifford A. 2001. ''Mediterranean Vegetables: a cook's ABC of vegetables and their preparation in Spain, France, Italy, Greece, Turkey, the Middle East, and north Africa with more than 200 authentic recipes for the home cook'' Boston, Massachusetts: Harvard Common Press, [https://books.google.com/books?id=tka838efZvkC & pg=PA52 page 52], {{ISBN|1-55832-196-9}} < /ref >  'mangel beet', < ref name= " Wright "  / >  'field beet', < ref > Raynbird, Hugh (1851)  " On the Cultivation of Mangold-wurzel or Field-beet "  ''Transactions of the Highland and Agricultural Society of Scotland''; New Series pp. & nbsp;534-38, [https://books.google.com/books?id=tEzOAAAAMAAJ & pg=PA534 page 534] < /ref >  and 'fodder beet'. It is a cultivated [[root vegetable]]. 
 
 It is a [[variety (botany)|variety]] of ''[[Beta vulgaris]]'', the same [[species]] that contains the [[beetroot|red beet]] (beetroot) and [[sugar beet]] varieties. The [[cultivar]] group is called the Crassa Group. < ref name=MMPND > {{Cite web|title=Sorting Beta names |url=http://www.plantnames.unimelb.edu.au/Sorting/Beta.html |work=Multilingual Multiscript Plant Name Database |publisher=The University of Melbourne |access-date=2016-08-17 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://www.webcitation.org/6Fu9TOBWl?url=http://www.plantnames.unimelb.edu.au/Sorting/Beta.html |archive-date=2013-04-15 }} < /ref >  Their large white, yellow or orange-yellow swollen roots were developed in the 18th century as a [[fodder]] crop for feeding [[livestock]].
