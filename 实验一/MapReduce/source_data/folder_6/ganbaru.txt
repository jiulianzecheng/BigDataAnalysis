{{nihongo|'''Ganbaru'''|頑張る|lit., stand firm}}, also romanized as '''gambaru''',  is a widely-used [[Japanese language|Japanese]] cultural concept. < ref > Allison, Anne. (1994). [https://books.google.com/books?id=6O_hLG3eQQUC & pg=PA119 ''Nightwork: Sexuality, Pleasure, and Corporate Masculinity in a Tokyo Hostess Club,'' p. 119]. < /ref >   It roughly means to slog on or stick to it through tough times. < ref > Kristoff, Nicholas. [https://www.nytimes.com/1997/09/26/world/shoichi-yokoi-82-is-dead-japan-soldier-hid-27-years.html?scp=2 & sq=ganbaru & st=nyt   " Shoichi Yokoi, 82, Is Dead; Japan Soldier Hid 27 Years, " ] ''New York Times'' (US). September 26, 1997; retrieved 2012-3-28. < /ref >  ''Ganbaru'' implies a kind of hard work which is defined by Japanese culture. 
 
 The word ''Ganbaru'' is often translated to mean  " doing one’s best " .  In practice, it means doing more than one's best. < ref > Frühstück, Sabine. (1998). [https://books.google.com/books?id=k_Cb7a6FQwwC & pg=PA104 ''The Culture of Japan as seen through its Leisure,'' p. 104]. < /ref >   The word emphasizes working with continued effort despite difficulties, failure, or distraction. < ref > Zeng, Kangmin. (1998). [https://books.google.com/books?id=wRWFBwgCwhsC & pg=PA224 ''Dragon gate: Competitive Examinations and Their Consequences,'' p. 224]. < /ref >   The term means  " toughing it out " . < ref > Kristoff, Nicholas D. [https://www.nytimes.com/1997/01/22/world/a-japanese-generation-haunted-by-its-past.html?scp=1 & sq=ganbaru & st=nyt & pagewanted=3   " A Japanese Generation Haunted by Its Past,:] ''New York Times'' (US). January 22, 1997; retrieved 2012-3-28. < /ref > 
 
 ''Ganbaru'' means to commit fully to a task and to bring that task to an end. < ref > Albach, Horst. (1994). [https://books.google.com/books?id=Xn8OVNDC1_EC & pg=PA388 ''Culture and Technical Innovation: a Cross-Cultural Analysis and Policy Recommendations,'' p. 388]. < /ref >    
 
 ''Ganbaru'' means an active process of doing something. It contrasts with a passive process. < ref > Haghirian, Parissa. [http://accjjournal.com/mastering-the-basics/2/  " Mastering The Basics, " ] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110709100904/http://accjjournal.com/mastering-the-basics/2/ |date=2011-07-09 }} [http://www.accj.or.jp/user/210/index/  American Chamber of Commerce in Japan] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110330112308/http://www.accj.or.jp/user/210/index/ |date=2011-03-30 }} (ACCJ), 15 February 2011; excerpt,  " Where ''ganbaru'' is an active process and requires people to do something to achieve their goals, ''gaman'' is passive and focuses more on enduring and not complaining. " < /ref >   
 
 ==History== 
 The term ''ganbaru'' developed from the related concept of ''gaman''. < ref > Hanabusa, George. (2003). [https://books.google.com/books?id=nawl2Z-uY1kC & pg=PA40 ''Second Chances Gospel,'' p. 40]. < /ref >    
 
 After the [[1995 Kobe earthquake]], the slogan  " ''Gambaro Kobe'' "  was used to encourage the people of the Kobe region. < ref > Davies, Roger J. ''et al.'' (2001). [https://books.google.com/books?id=TW7lHYwXhS4C & pg=PA84 ''The Japanese Mind: Understanding Contemporary Japanese Culture,'' p. 84]. < /ref >    
 
 After the [[2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami]], ''ganbaru'' was one of the most commonly heard expressions. < ref > [http://www.11alive.com/news/local/story.aspx?storyid=183076 & provider=top   " U.S. donations not rushing to Japan, " ] 11Alive News (US). March 17, 2011; excerpt,  " Devin Stewart, a senior director at the Japan Society in New York City, said,  " Suffering and persevering is a type of virtue in Japan ... the ability to persevere and remain calm under difficult situations. Among the most commonly heard expressions there, are ''gaman'', to persevere or tough it out; ''gambaru'', to do your best, to be strong; and ''shoganai'' (''shikata ga nai''), it cannot be helped, which expresses a sense of fatalism .... " < /ref >
