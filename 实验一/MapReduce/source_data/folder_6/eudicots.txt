 '''Eudicots''' or '''eudicotyledons''' are a group of [[flowering plant]]s. They are one of two major [[clade]]s, the ''non-magnoliid dicots''. Their sister group is the [[Magnoliids]], a much smaller group. < ref > Endress, Peter K. 2002. Morphology and Angiosperm systematics in the molecular era. ''Botanical Review''. Structural botany in systematics: a symposium in memory of William C. Dickison. '''68''' (4): 545â€“570. [http://doc.rero.ch/record/313995/files/12229_2008_Article_684545.pdf] < /ref > 
 
 The [[Dicotyledon|dicot]]s and [[Monocotyledon|monocot]]s are the flowering plants which took over in the long [[Cretaceous]] period. They largely replaced the earlier types of plants. Of the old forests of the [[Mesozoic]] [[era]], only the [[conifers]] are still common today. 
 
 The big groups of eudicots are the [[Rosids|rosid]]s and [[asterids]] (together 70% of angiosperms). [[Monocotyledon|Monocots]] make up most of the rest. < ref > Soltis, Douglas E.; Soltis, Pamela S.; Endress, Peter K.; Chase, Mark W. 2005. ''Phylogeny and evolution of Angiosperms''. Sunderland, MA: Sinauer Associates. ISBN 9780878938179 < /ref > 
 
 A few examples of eudicots are [[forget-me-not]], [[cabbage]], [[apple]], [[dandelion]], [[buttercup]], [[maple]] and [[macadamia]]. 
 
 The tree forms of [[flowering plant]]s evolved during the [[Cretaceous]] period. These began to displace the [[conifers]] during the [[Tertiary]] era (66 to 2 million years ago). Forests covered much of the globe before the climate cooled. < ref name=RFS > {{cite web |url=http://www.rfs.org.uk/learning/forestry-knowledge-hub/trees-biology/tree-evolution/ |title=Tree evolution |year=2012 |work=Tree Biology |publisher=Royal Forestry Society |access-date=2012-07-25 |archive-date=2016-10-23 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161023054737/http://www.rfs.org.uk/learning/forestry-knowledge-hub/trees-biology/tree-evolution/ |url-status=dead }} < /ref >
