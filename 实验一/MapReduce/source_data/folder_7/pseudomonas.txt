 Pseudomonas is a [[gram-negative bacteria]]. It is a [[genus]] belonging to the [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudomonadaceae Pseudomonadaceae] family. There are about 144 [[species]] in this genus. < ref name= " 144 species " > {{cite journal |last1=Gomila |first1=Margarita |last2=Peña |first2=Arantxa |last3=Mulet |first3=Magdalena |last4=Lalucat |first4=Jorge |last5=García-Valdés |first5=Elena |title=Phylogenomics and systematics in Pseudomonas |journal=Frontiers in Microbiology |date=2015 |volume=6 |page=214 |doi=10.3389/fmicb.2015.00214 |pmid=26074881 |pmc=4447124 |issn=1664-302X |doi-access=free }} < /ref >  Most of the Pseudomonas species are [[Saprophyte|saprotrophic]], meaning they release enzymes that break down big substances into small ones.  
 
 ==History== 
 Pseudomonas is one of the earliest found bacteria. It was first called 'Bacterium aeruginosm' by Schroter in 1872. However, in 1894, German botanist [[:en:Walter_Migula|Walter Migula]] classified it as [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa|''Pseudomonas aeruginosa'']]. In Greek, pseudo means false and monas means “unit”. < ref > {{cite journal |title=Etymologia: Pseudomonas |journal=Emerging Infectious Diseases |date=August 2012 |volume=18 |issue=8 |pages=1241 |doi=10.3201/eid1808.ET1808 |s2cid=26621145 }} < /ref >  This bacteria is associated with many chronic infections and is considered dangerous due to its resistance to many [[antibiotic]]s. < ref > {{cite journal |last1=Rudra |first1=Bashudev |last2=Duncan |first2=Louise |last3=Shah |first3=Ajit J. |last4=Shah |first4=Haroun N. |last5=Gupta |first5=Radhey S. |title=Phylogenomic and comparative genomic studies robustly demarcate two distinct clades of Pseudomonas aeruginosa strains: proposal to transfer the strains from an outlier clade to a novel species Pseudomonas paraeruginosa sp. nov. |url=https://www.microbiologyresearch.org/content/journal/ijsem/10.1099/ijsem.0.005542?crawler=true |journal=International Journal of Systematic and Evolutionary Microbiology |access-date=7 July 2023 |pages=005542 |doi=10.1099/ijsem.0.005542 |date=2022|volume=72 |issue=11 |pmid=36355412 |s2cid=253445493 }} < /ref >  There has been an increase in the number of species discovered year by year. In 2013, 10 species have been found and 6 more in the subsequent year. < ref name= " 2014 year article "  / >  Pseudomonas was well-classified 100 years later by a Canadian scientist, Roger Stanier.  < ref > {{cite journal |last1=Stanier |first1=RY |last2=Palleroni |first2=NJ |last3=Doudoroff |first3=M |title=The aerobic pseudomonads: a taxonomic study. |journal=Journal of General Microbiology |date=May 1966 |volume=43 |issue=2 |pages=159–271 |doi=10.1099/00221287-43-2-159 |pmid=5963505}} < /ref >  The classification was based on the physiological and biochemical properties. < ref name= " 2014 year article " > {{cite journal |last1=Gomila |first1=M |last2=Peña |first2=A |last3=Mulet |first3=M |last4=Lalucat |first4=J |last5=García-Valdés |first5=E |title=Phylogenomics and systematics in Pseudomonas. |journal=Frontiers in Microbiology |date=2015 |volume=6 |pages=214 |doi=10.3389/fmicb.2015.00214 |pmid=26074881 |pmc=4447124 |doi-access=free }} < /ref > 
 
 ==Phylogeny== 
 The basic tool which is used to classify Pseudomonas is the 16S rRNA gene.  < ref > {{cite journal |last1=Saati-Santamaría |first1=Z |last2=Peral-Aranega |first2=E |last3=Velázquez |first3=E |last4=Rivas |first4=R |last5=García-Fraile |first5=P |date=16 August 2021 |title=Phylogenomic Analyses of the Genus Pseudomonas Lead to the Rearrangement of Several Species and the Definition of New Genera. |journal=Biology |volume=10 |issue=8 |page=782 |doi=10.3390/biology10080782 |pmc=8389581 |pmid=34440014 |doi-access=free}} < /ref >  However, it is hard to differentiate closely related Pseudomonas species, thus [[Gene|genes]] such as atpD, gyrB, rpoB, recA, and rpoD are also used in the [[taxonomy]] of Pseudomonas. < ref > {{cite journal |last1=Mulet |first1=Magdalena |last2=Gomila |first2=Margarita |last3=Scotta |first3=Claudia |last4=Sánchez |first4=David |last5=Lalucat |first5=Jorge |last6=García-Valdés |first6=Elena |date=1 October 2012 |title=Concordance between whole-cell matrix-assisted laser-desorption/ionization time-of-flight mass spectrometry and multilocus sequence analysis approaches in species discrimination within the genus Pseudomonas |journal=Systematic and Applied Microbiology |volume=35 |issue=7 |pages=455–464 |doi=10.1016/j.syapm.2012.08.007 |pmid=23140936}} < /ref > [[File:Phylogenetic tree - Pseudomonas teessidea.png|thumb|left|upright|300px| Phylogenetic tree of Pseudomonas with Pseudomonas teessidea highlighted in red]]Using gyrB and rpoD in phylogenetic analysis, Pseudomonas came from two predominant clusters; intrageneric cluster I (IGC I) and intrageneric cluster II (IGC II). < ref > {{Cite journal |last1=Yamamoto |first1=Satoshi |last2=Kasai |first2=Hiroaki |last3=Arnold |first3=Dawn L. |last4=Jackson |first4=Robert W. |last5=Vivian |first5=Alan |last6=Harayama |first6=Shigeaki |date=2000-10-01 |title=Phylogeny of the genus Pseudomonas: intrageneric structure reconstructed from the nucleotide sequences of gyrB and rpoD genes The GenBank accession numbers for the sequences determined in this work are: gyrB, D37926, D37297, D86005–D86019 and AB039381–AB039492; rpoD, D86020–D86036 and AB039493–AB039624. |url=https://www.microbiologyresearch.org/content/journal/micro/10.1099/00221287-146-10-2385 |journal=Microbiology |language=en |volume=146 |issue=10 |pages=2385–2394 |doi=10.1099/00221287-146-10-2385 |issn=1350-0872 |pmid=11021915}} < /ref >   
 
 IGC I consists of two sub clusters, the ‘''Pseudomonas aeruginosa'' complex’  comprises of ''Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Pseudomonas alcaligenes, Pseudomonas citronellolis, Pseudomonas mendocina, Pseudomonas oleovorans'' and ''Pseudomonas pseudoalcaligenes,'' and the ‘''Pseudomonas stutzeri'' complex’, comprises of ''Pseudomonas balearica'' and ''Pseudomonas stutzeri''.  
 
 IGC II consists of three subclusters, the ‘''Pseudomonas syringae'' complex’, the ‘''Pseudomonas fluorescens'' complex’ and the ‘''Pseudomonas putida'' complex’.  The ‘''Pseudomonas syringae'' complex’ are phytopathogens and pathovars. The former encompasses ''Pseudomonas amygdali, Pseudomonas caricapapayae, Pseudomonas cichorii, Pseudomonas ficuserectae'' and ''Pseudomonas viridiflava''  and the latter consists of ''Pseudomonas savastanoi'' and ''Pseudomonas syringae''. The ‘''Pseudomonas fluorescens'' complex’ is divided into two subpopulations, the ‘''Pseudomonas fluorescens'' lineage’ and the ‘''Pseudomonas chlororaphis'' lineage’. The ‘''Pseudomonas fluorescens'' lineage’ contains ''Pseudomonas fluorescens'' biotypes A, B and C, ''Pseudomonas azotoformans, Pseudomonas marginalis'' pathovars, ''Pseudomonas mucidolens'', ''Pseudomonas synxantha'' and ''Pseudomonas tolaasii'', while the ‘''Pseudomonas chlororaphis'' lineage’ includes ''Pseudomonas chlororaphis, Pseudomonas agarici, Pseudomonas asplenii'' and ''Pseudomonas corrugata''. The ‘''Pseudomonas putida'' complex’ comprises of ''Pseudomonas putida'' and ''Pseudomonas fulva''. < ref > {{Cite journal |last1=Yamamoto |first1=Satoshi |last2=Kasai |first2=Hiroaki |last3=Arnold |first3=Dawn L. |last4=Jackson |first4=Robert W. |last5=Vivian |first5=Alan |last6=Harayama |first6=Shigeaki |date=October 2000 |title=Phylogeny of the genus Pseudomonas: intrageneric structure reconstructed from the nucleotide sequences of gyrB and rpoD genes |url=https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11021915 |journal=Microbiology (Reading, England) |volume=146 ( Pt 10) |pages=2385–2394 |doi=10.1099/00221287-146-10-2385 |issn=1350-0872 |pmid=11021915}} < /ref > 
 
 == Clinical manifestations == 
 More than 25 species of Pseudomonas cause [[Disease|diseases]] in humans. Species such as ''Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Pseudomonas fluorescens, Pseudomonas putida, Pseudomonas cepacia, Pseudomonas stutzeri, Pseudomonas maltophilia,'' and ''Pseudomonas putrefaciens'' are said to cause opportunistic infections in humans.  < ref > {{cite book |last1=Iglewski |first1=Barbara H. |title=Medical Microbiology |date=1996 |publisher=University of Texas Medical Branch at Galveston |isbn=978-0-9631172-1-2 |pages=Chapter 27 |edition=4th |url=https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK8326/ |accessdate=24 June 2023 |chapter=Pseudomonas}} < /ref >  Opportunistic infections more commonly affect people with weak immune systems compared to the ones with stronger immune system. < ref name= " oppurtunistic infections " > {{cite web |title=What is an Opportunistic Infection? {{!}} NIH |url=https://hivinfo.nih.gov/understanding-hiv/fact-sheets/what-opportunistic-infection#:~:text=an%20opportunistic%20infection%3F-,Opportunistic%20infections%20(OIs)%20are%20infections%20that%20occur%20more%20often%20or,%2C%20fungi%2C%20and%20parasites). |website=hivinfo.nih.gov |accessdate=24 June 2023 |language=en}} < /ref >  Only two of the species are known to cause specific disease. These are ''Pseudomonas mallei'' and ''Pseudomonas pseudomallei'' which cause glanders and melioidosis respectively. However, the most common species involved in clinical problems is ''Pseudomonas aeruginosa''. < ref > {{cite book |last1=Iglewski |first1=Barbara H. |title=Medical Microbiology |date=1996 |publisher=University of Texas Medical Branch at Galveston |isbn=978-0-9631172-1-2 |pages=Chapter 27 |edition=4th |url=https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK8326/ |accessdate=24 June 2023 |chapter=Pseudomonas}} < /ref >  The common clinical problems caused by ''Pseudomonas aeruginosa'' is shown in the following bullet points. < ref name= " :1 " > {{Cite web |title=Pseudomonas Infection Clinical Presentation: History, Physical, Causes |url=https://emedicine.medscape.com/article/970904-clinical |access-date=2023-07-19 |website=emedicine.medscape.com}} < /ref >
