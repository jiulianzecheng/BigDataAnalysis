 A '''Macaron'''  < ref > {{cite web|title=Definition of macaron in English|url=https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/macaron|website=Oxford Dictionaries|access-date=16 April 2018|archive-date=16 April 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180416200630/https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/macaron|url-status=dead}} < /ref > < ref > {{cite web|title=Definition of macaron|url=https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/macaron|website=Merriam-Webster|access-date=16 April 2018}} < /ref > < ref > {{cite web|title=Definition of macaroon in English|url=https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/macaroon|website=Oxford Dictionaries|access-date=16 April 2018|archive-date=16 April 2018|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180416201218/https://en.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/macaroon|url-status=dead}} < /ref > < ref > {{cite web|title=Definition of macaroon|url=https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/macaroon|website=Merriam-Webster|access-date=16 April 2018}} < /ref >  is a sweet [[meringue]]-based [[confection]] made with [[egg white]], icing sugar, granulated sugar, [[almond meal]], and [[food colouring]].  
 
 The Macaron have been introduced in France by the Italian chef of queen [[Catherine de Medici]] during the [[Renaissance]]. Since the 19th century, a macaron is shown with a [[ganache]], buttercream or [[jam]] filling sandwiched between two cookies. It is moist and easily melts in the mouth.
