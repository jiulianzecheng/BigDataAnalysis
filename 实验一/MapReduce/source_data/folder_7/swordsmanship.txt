 '''Swordsmanship''' refers to the [[skill]]s of a ''[[sword]]sman''.  It is a modern [[wikt:term|term]] mainly used to refer to [[small sword]] fencing. But it can also be applied to any [[martial art]] involving the use of a sword. The [[English language|English]] word  " swordsman "  is parallel to the [[Latin language|Latin]] word ''[[gladiator]]''. < ref name= " etymology " > Douglas Harper, ''[http://www.etymonline.com/index.php?search=swordsman & searchmode=none Etymology Online]'' (2014), accessed 3 August 2014 < /ref >  This was a professional fighter who fought against other fighters and others for the [[entertainment]] of [[wikt:spectator|spectators]] in the [[Roman Empire|Roman]] [[Colosseum]]. The word ''gladiator'' itself comes from the Latin word ''[[gladius]]'', meaning  " sword " . < ref name= " etymology " / >   
 
 ==Noted swordsmen == 
 
 Japan's greatest swordsman, [[Miyamoto Musashi]] won sixty [[duel]]s. < ref name=Evangelista318 > Nick Evangelista, ''The Encyclopedia of the Sword'' (Westport, CT: Greenwood Press, 1995), p. 318 < /ref >  His classic book on swordsmanship is called the [[Book of Five Rings]], written about 1645. < ref name=Evangelista318/ > 
 
 [[William Marshal, 1st Earl of Pembroke]] was called  " the greatest knight who ever lived " . He was one of [[England]]'s greatest swordsmen. < ref > {{cite web |url=http://famous101.com/famous-swordsmen-in-history |title=Famous Swordsmen in History |author= < !--Staff writer(s); no by-line.-- >  |date=4 June 2013 |website= |publisher=Famous Things, People and Events |accessdate=3 August 2014 }} < /ref > 
 
 [[Guy Chabot]], Seigneur de Jarnac was famous as [[France]]'s finest swordsman. He won by a slashing blow to his opponent's [[buttocks]] so he could no longer stand. Winning a duel using this method has since been called a ''coup de Jarnac''. < ref > Nick Evangelista, ''The Encyclopedia of the Sword'' (Westport, CT: Greenwood Press, 1995), p. 320 < /ref > 
 
 [[Italo Santelli]] was a great [[Italians|Italian]] [[fencing]] master (teacher). < ref name=Evangelista527 > Nick Evangelista, ''The Encyclopedia of the Sword'' (Westport, CT: Greenwood Press, 1995), p. 527 < /ref >  His fencing styles made Italians the best swordsmen in [[Europe]] by the end of the [[19th century]]. < ref name=Evangelista527/ >  He established and taught fencing in [[Hungary]] for over fifty years. By the [[20th century]] his Hungarian fencers were the best in Europe. < ref name=Evangelista527/ >  He was given a [[knighthood]] for his contributions to the [[sport]] in Hungary. < ref name=Evangelista527/ >
