 '''Otaku''' is a [[Japanese language|Japanese]] [[slang]] word, deriving from the honorific  " お宅 < ref > {{Cite web|url=https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E3%81%8A%E3%81%9F%E3%81%8F|title=おたく - Wiktionary|website=en.wiktionary.org|language=en|accessdate=2017-03-27}} < /ref > "  (otaku), meaning  " your house " , that has come to mean someone who obsessively exerts time, money, and energy into a [[hobby]]. Otaku have a deep, complex knowledge around a particular subject or area of study, and is oftentimes (esp. in [[Western world|the West]]) used to refer to avid consumers of [[anime]] and [[manga]]. The term can also be compared with [[Hikikomori]]''.'' 
 
 In Japan, ''otaku'' is generally regarded as an offensive word, due to the negative cultural perception of withdrawal from society. However, the otaku philosophy of living has gained traction among Japanese youth and adolescents, likely as a reaction to the established culture of intense work and academic studies. Under 30’s have developed a neo-otaku identity with more positive associations of being a [[geek]] or [[:en:Nerd|nerd]]. < ref > {{Cite news|url=http://adage.com/article/guest-columnists/japanese-youth-otaku-means/144414/|title=Why Japanese Youth Want to Be 'Otaku' and What It Means {{!}} Guest Colum…|date=2017-03-27|newspaper=archive.is|accessdate=2017-03-27|archive-date=2017-03-27|archive-url=https://archive.today/20170327031325/http://adage.com/article/guest-columnists/japanese-youth-otaku-means/144414/|url-status=bot: unknown}} < /ref > < ref > {{Cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2015/feb/22/japan-long-hours-work-culture-overwork-paid-holiday-law|title=Clocking off: Japan calls time on long-hours work culture|last=McCurry|first=Justin|date=2015-02-22|newspaper=The Guardian|accessdate=2017-03-27|language=en-GB|issn=0261-3077}} < /ref >  In the West, the word usually describes an anime or manga  " fanatic "  and is not typically perceived as derogatory.
