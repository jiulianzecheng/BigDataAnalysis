 '''Fustanella''' ({{lang-el|φουστανέλλα}}; {{lang-sq|fustanellë/fustanella}}) is a pleated, or folded, [[skirt]] like a Scottish [[kilt]]. It is worn by men from different countries in [[Southeast Europe]]. 
 
 ==History== 
 [[File:Nympholept.jpg|thumb|200px|left|Statue in the Vari Cave showing the Greek stonecutter Archedemus wearing a fustanella-like garment (5th century BC).]] 
 [[File:12th century Greek Warrior Fustanella.JPG|thumb|200px|right|Byzantine pottery fragments from Corinth showing Greek warriors wearing the fustanella (12th century AD). < ref name=Morgan/ > ]] 
 
 The fustanella originates from [[Ancient Greece|ancient Greek]] [[clothing]] like the ''chiton'' (or tunic) and the ''chitonium'' (or short military tunic). < ref > {{harvnb|Smithsonian Institution|Mouseio Benakē|1959|p=8}}; {{harvnb|Fox|1977|p=56}}. < /ref >  An ancient Greek [[statue]] in [[Attica]] shows a stonecutter named Archedemus wearing a folded skirt like the fustanella. < ref > {{harvnb|Weller|1903|pp=271–273}}. < /ref >  The [[Ancient Rome|ancient Roman]] [[toga]] may have also impacted the development of the fustanella. < ref name= " Notopoulos 1964 114 " > {{harvnb|Notopoulos|1964|p=114}}. < /ref > 
 
 In the [[Byzantine Empire]], the fustanella was worn and called the ''podea'' ({{lang-el|ποδέα}}). < ref > {{harvnb|Notopoulos|1964|pp=110, 122}}. < /ref > < ref name=Kazhdan47 > {{harvnb|Kazhdan|1991|loc= " Akritic Imagery " , p. 47}}. < /ref >  It was commonly used in Greek lands as early as the 12th century AD. < ref name=Morgan > {{harvnb|Morgan|1942|pp=132–133}}. < /ref >  In [[Byzantine art]] and music, the wearer of the ''podea'' was either a hero or a Greek warrior defending the empire's [[border]]s. < ref name= " Kazhdan47 "  / > < ref > {{harvnb|Morgan|1942|pp=133, 317–318, 333}}; {{harvnb|Notopoulos|1964|pp=110, 113}}. < /ref >  The fustanella was part of a military outfit consisting of [[bow and arrow|bows]], [[sword]]s, [[Battle axe|battle-axes]] and [[armor]] (corselet or [[chain mail]]). < ref name= " Morgan "  / > < ref name= " Notopoulos 1964 114 "  / >  In the [[Ottoman Empire]], the fustanella was worn by Greek [[Guerilla warfare|guerillas]] like the klephts and the armatoloi. < ref > {{harvnb|Ethniko Historiko Mouseio (Greece), Maria Lada-Minōtou, I. K. Mazarakēs Ainian, Diana Gangadē, and Historikē kai Ethnologikē Hetaireia tēs Hellados|1993|p=xxx}}; {{harvnb|Notopoulos|1964|pp=113–115}}; {{harvnb|Smithsonian Institution|Mouseio Benakē|1959|p=8}}. < /ref >  In [[Albania]], the fustanella is first mentioned in 1335 in a list of items taken from a [[sailor]] at the [[port]] of the Drin River. < ref > {{harvnb|Gjergji|2004|p=16}}. < /ref >   
 
 The Albanian fustanella is based on the Greek fustanella. < ref > {{harvnb|Rupert|1842|pp=356–357}}. < /ref >  But the difference is in the number of pleats. The  " Bridegroom's coat "  is a Greek fustanella with two-hundred pleats that a [[bride]] would buy as a wedding gift for her [[groom]]. < ref > {{harvnb|Smithsonian Institution|Mouseio Benakē|1959|p=31}}; {{harvnb|Fox|1977|p=56}}. < /ref >  The Albanian fustanella has around sixty pleats or usually a moderate amount of folds. < ref > {{harvnb|Konitza|1957|pp=85–86}}. < /ref > [[File:Ottomcostumes1873_planche19_janina.jpg|thumb|Albanian costumes with the fustanella from [[Ioannina|Yanina]] in 1873. < ref > {{cite web|last=Elsie|first=Robert|title=Ottoman Costumes 1873|url=http://albanianphotography.net/OttomCostumes/|website=albanianphotography.net}} < /ref > ]]
