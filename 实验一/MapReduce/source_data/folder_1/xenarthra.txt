 The '''Xenarthra''' is a superorder of [[mammal]]s. It is a group of [[placental mammal]]s (infraclass [[Eutheria]]). They live at present only in the [[Americas]], and are [[anteater]]s, tree [[sloth]]s, and [[armadillo]]s.  
 
 Their origins can be traced back as far as the [[Palaeogene]] (about 60-65 million years ago (mya), shortly after the [[Mesozoic]]) in South America. < ref > {{cite journal|last=Archibald|first=J. David|title=Timing and biogeography of the eutherian radiation: fossils and molecules compared|journal=Molecular Phylogenetics and Evolution|date=August 2003|volume=28|issue=2|pages=350–359|doi=10.1016/s1055-7903(03)00034-4|pmid=12878471|url=https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12878471}} < /ref >   Xenarthrans developed and diversified extensively in [[South America]] during its long period of isolation, invaded the [[Antilles]] by the early [[Miocene]], and then spread to [[Central America|Central]] and [[North America]] as part of the [[Great American Interchange]]. < ref > {{cite journal|last=Woodburne|first=Michael|title=The Great American biotic interchange: dispersals, tectonics, climate, sea level, and holding pens|journal=Journal of Mammalian Evolution|year=2010|volume=17|issue=4|pages=245–264|doi=10.1007/s10914-010-9144-8|pmid=21125025|pmc=2987556|url=https://dx.doi.org/10.1007/s10914-010-9144-8|accessdate=18 October 2011}}{{Dead link|date=January 2021 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }} < /ref >   
 
 Nearly all of the once abundant [[megafauna|big]] xenarthrans, such as [[ground sloth]]s, [[Glyptodontidae|glyptodonts]], and [[Pampatheriidae|pampatheres]] went [[extinct]] at the end of the [[Pleistocene]]. 
 
 Xenarthrans differ from other placental mammals in several ways. The name Xenarthra means 'strange joints', and was chosen because their vertebral joints have extra articulations and are unlike those of any other mammals. The males lack external [[testicle]]s, which are instead placed between the bladder and the rectum. < ref > {{cite journal|title=The evolutionary history of testicular externalization and the origin of the scrotum|journal=J.
