 In [[Norse mythology]], the '''einherjar''' (lone-fighters < ref > John Lindow, ''Norse Mythology: A Guide to Gods, Heroes, Rituals, and Beliefs'' (Oxford; New York: Oxford University Press, 2002), p. 104 < /ref > ) are [[warrior]]s who have died in [[battle]] and are brought to [[Valhalla]] by [[valkyrie]]s. < ref > Kathleen N Daly; Marian Rengel, ''Norse Mythology A to Z'' (New York: Chelsea House Publishers, 2010), p. 111 < /ref >  In Valhalla, the einherjar eat [[meat]] from the [[boar]] called [[Sæhrímnir]]. < ref name=Chaillu427 > Paul Belloni Du Chaillu, ''The Viking Age'' (New York: C. Scribner's Sons, 1890), p. 427 < /ref >  Every day he is boiled and every night he becomes whole again. < ref name=Chaillu427/ >  They drink [[mead]] provided by a [[goat]] named [[Heiðrún]]. < ref name=Chaillu427/ >  Every day they dress for battle and go out and fight each other. < ref > ''Collier's new Encyclopedia'' (New York: P.F. Collier, 1921), p. 154 < /ref >  This is to prepare for the events of [[Ragnarök]] (the end of the world). < ref > Charles Russell Coulter; Patricia Turner, ''Encyclopedia of Ancient Deities'' (New York; Oxford: Routledge, 2012), p. 164 < /ref > 
 
 The einherjar are written about in the ''[[Prose Edda]]'' < ref > ''Ideology and Power in the Viking and Middle Ages'', ed. Gro Steinsland (Leiden; Boston: Brill, 2011), p. 297 < /ref >  by [[Snorri Sturluson]]. Also in the poem written in the [[13th century]] ''[[Hákonarmál]]'', by [[Eyvindr skáldaspillir]].
