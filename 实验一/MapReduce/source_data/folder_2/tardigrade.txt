 '''Tardigrades''' ('water bears') are [[Microscope|microscopic]], segmented [[animal]]s. < ref name= " Neuman " > {{cite journal|last=Neuman|first=Yair|date=October 2006|title=Cryptobiosis: A new theoretical perspective|url=https://archive.org/details/sim_progress-in-biophysics-and-molecular-biology_2006-10_92_2/page/258|journal=Progress in Biophysics and Molecular Biology|publisher=Elsevier|volume=92|issue=2|pages=258–267|doi=10.1016/j.pbiomolbio.2005.11.001|pmid=16380155}} < /ref >  They live in water. They are members of the [[tactopod]] [[phylum]] '''Tardigrada''', part of the [[superphylum]] [[Ecdysozoa]]. They are also known as '''water bears''' or '''moss piglets'''. < ref name= " American Scientist " > {{cite web |url=https://www.americanscientist.org/article/tardigrades |title=Tardigrades |publisher=American Scientist |accessdate=2018-04-13 |first=William |last=Miller |date=2017-02-06 |archive-date=2018-04-14 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180414091816/https://www.americanscientist.org/article/tardigrades |url-status=live }} < /ref > < ref name= " WRD-20140321 " > {{cite magazine |last=Simon |first=Matt |title=Absurd Creature of the Week: the incredible Ccritter that's tough enough to survive in the vacuum of space |url=https://www.wired.com/wiredscience/2014/03/absurd-creature-week-water-bear/ |date=21 March 2014 |magazine=[[Wired (magazine)|Wired]] |accessdate=2014-03-21 |archive-date=2014-03-25 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140325115059/http://www.wired.com/wiredscience/2014/03/absurd-creature-week-water-bear |url-status=live }} < /ref >   
 
 Tardigrades were first [[wikt:describe|described]] in 1773. < ref name= " Riffenburgh " > {{cite book| last = Riffenburgh| first = Beau| title = Encyclopedia of the Antarctic| edition = illustrated| volume = 1| date = 2007| publisher = CRC Press| isbn = 978-0-415-97024-2| page = 983 }} < /ref >  Their name means  " slow stepper " . There are more than 1000 different [[species]] of tardigrade. < ref name= " Five kingdoms " > {{cite book| last1 = Margulis| first1 = Lynn| last2 = Schwartz| first2 = Karlene V.| title = Five kingdoms: an illustrated guide to the phyla of life on earth| url = https://archive.org/details/fivekingdomsillu00marg_0| edition = illustrated| date = 1998| publisher = Elsevier| isbn = 978-0-7167-3027-9| page = [https://archive.org/details/fivekingdomsillu00marg_0/page/324 324] }} < /ref > 
 
 Tardigrades have a [[cylinder|cylindrical]] shape with four [[segment]]s, each with two legs. Each leg has little [[claw]]s. < ref name= " Five kingdoms " / >  The biggest adults may reach a body length of 1.2 & nbsp;mm, the smallest below 0.1 & nbsp;mm. Freshly hatched [[larvae]] may be smaller than 0.05 & nbsp;mm. < ref name= " Riffenburgh " / >  Tardigrades feed on [[plant]] [[cell]]s by [[wikt:penetrate|penetrating]] the [[cell wall]] and eating what is inside. Some tardigrades are [[carnivores]]. < ref name= " Encyclopædia Britannica " > {{cite web|url=http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/583460/tardigrade|title=Tardigrade (animal)|publisher=Encyclopædia Britannica|accessdate=2011-01-02|archive-date=2011-01-23|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110123020802/http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/583460/tardigrade|url-status=live}} < /ref > < ref name= " Five kingdoms " / > 
 
 Tardigrades are [[eutelic]]: all adult tardigrades of the same species have the same number of [[Cell (biology)|cells]]. Some species have as many as 40,000 cells in each adult, while others have far fewer. < ref name= " Seki98 " > Seki, Kunihiro; Toyoshima, Masato 1998. Preserving tardigrades under pressure. ''Nature'' '''395''' (6705): 853–854. [http://www.nature.com/nature/journal/v395/n6705/full/395853a0.html Preserving tardigrades under pressure  & #124; Nature] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210214140201/https://www.nature.com/articles/27576 |date=2021-02-14 }} < /ref > < ref > Kinchin, Ian M. 1994. ''The biology of tardigrades''. Ashgate Publishing. < /ref > 
 
 Tardigrades can be found in many [[habitat]]s: in [[moss]], < ref name= " Encyclopædia Britannica " / >  [[freshwater]], < ref name= " Encyclopædia Britannica " / >  the [[Himalayas]], < ref name= " Riffenburgh " / >  and the [[ocean]]. < ref name= " Riffenburgh " / >  They are one of the few animals that can be found on the highest mountains and the deepest seas. < ref name= " Riffenburgh " / >  About 83% of the known species live on land, the other 17% live in water. < ref name= " Five kingdoms " / >
