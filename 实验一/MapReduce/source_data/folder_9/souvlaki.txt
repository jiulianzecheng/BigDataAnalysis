 '''Souvlaki''' ({{lang-el|σουβλάκι}}, {{lang|el-Latn|souvláki}}, {{IPA-el|suˈvlaci|}}; plural: {{lang|el|σουβλάκια}}, {{lang|el-Latn|souvlákia}}) is a [[Greek food]] made up of small pieces of [[meat]] and sometimes [[vegetable]]s grilled on a skewer or spit. It is often eaten straight off the skewer while still hot. Souvlaki can be served with or inside a rolled [[pita bread|pita]], typically with [[lemon]], [[sauces]], vegetables like sliced [[tomato]] and [[onion]], and fried [[potato]]es. The meats used are [[pork]], [[Chicken (food)|chicken]], [[beef]], and [[Lamb and mutton|lamb]]. 
 
 ==Name== 
 The word ''souvlaki'' is a [[diminutive]] of the [[Greek language|Greek]] word {{transl|grc|souvla}} ({{lang|grc|σούβλα}} meaning  " skewer " ) itself borrowed from the [[Latin language|Latin]] word {{lang|la|subula}}. < ref > Georgios Babiniotis, ''Λεξικό της Νεας Ελληνικής Γλώσσας'', ''s.v.''; Andriotis et al., Λεξικό της Κοινής Νεοελληνικής, [http://www.greek-language.gr/greekLang/modern_greek/tools/lexica/triantafyllides/search.html?lq=%CF%83%CE%BF%CF%85%CE%B2%CE%BB%CE%AC%CE%BA%CE%B9 ''s.v.'' σουβλάκι], [http://www.greek-language.gr/greekLang/modern_greek/tools/lexica/triantafyllides/search.html?lq=%CF%83%CE%BF%CF%8D%CE%B2%CE%BB%CE%B1 ''s.v.'' σούβλα] < /ref > < ref > {{harvnb|Sophocles|2004|p=[https://books.google.com/books?id=k3YDBwAAQBAJ & pg=PA1000 1000]}}. < /ref > 
 
 ==History== 
 [[File:Akrotiri terracotta firedogs with zoomorphic finials.jpg|thumb|Pair of firedogs with slots for holding skewers, 17th century BC, [[Akrotiri (prehistoric city)|Akrotiri]].]] 
 
 In Greek culture, the cooking of meat on skewers goes back to the [[Bronze Age]]. < ref name=Hruby > {{harvnb|Hruby|2017|loc= " Souvlaki trays " , pp. 23–25}}. < /ref >  Sets of cooking supports made of [[stone]] were found on the Greek island of [[Santorini]]. < ref name=Eptakili/ >  They were used by the natives to cook souvlaki,  " a popular delicacy in Santorini back in 2000 BC " , before the [[volcanic eruption]] on the island of Thera in the 17th century BC. < ref name=Eptakili > {{cite web|last=Eptakili|first=Tassoula|title=Prehistoric Gastronomy: Dietary habits of a long-lost era|website=Greece Is|date=9 October 2015|access-date=21 February 2016|url=http://www.greece-is.com/prehistoric-gastronomy-2/}} < /ref >  There are dents in the stone cooking supports likely used for holding skewers and the line of holes in the base allowed the [[coal]]s to be supplied with [[air]]. < ref > [https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Akrotiri_terracotta_firedogs_with_zoomorphic_finials.jpg Utilitarian terracotta object, Museum of Cycladic Culture, Akrotiri excavation artifacts, Santorini, Cyclades, Hellas (Greece)]. < /ref >   
 
 In [[Mycenaean Greece]],  " souvlaki trays "  were found in Gla, [[Mycenae]], and Pylos. < ref name=Hruby/ >  The  " souvlaki trays " , or portable grills, used by the Mycenaean Greeks were rectangular [[ceramic]] pans that sat below skewers of meat. < ref name=Hruby/ >  It is unclear whether these trays were put directly over a [[fire]] or if the pans held hot coals like a portable barbecue pit. < ref name=Hruby/ > < ref > {{cite web|last=Gannon|first=Megan|title=Ancient Greeks Used Portable Grills at Their Picnics|website=Live Science|date=8 January 2014|access-date=5 February 2020|url=http://www.livescience.com/42414-ancient-cooking-mycenaeans-portable-grills.html}} < /ref >  Spit supports used for cooking food appear to  " continue in use into the [[Archaic Greece|Early Iron Age]] at Nichoria. " < ref name=Hruby/ >  In Greek literature, the ''[[Iliad]]'' of [[Homer]] mentions pieces of meat cooked on spits called ''obelos'' (ὀβελός). < ref > Homer. ''Iliad'', 1.465. < /ref >  ''Obelos'' is also mentioned in the works of [[Aristophanes]], < ref > Aristophanes. ''Acharnians'' 1007, ''Clouds'' 178, ''Wasps'' 354, ''Birds'' 388, 672. < /ref >  [[Xenophon]], < ref > Xenophon. ''Hellenica'', HG3.3.7. < /ref >  [[Aristotle]], < ref > Aristotle. ''Politics'', 1324b19. < /ref >  and others. < ref > Homer. ''Iliad'', [https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Hom.+Il.+1.465 & fromdoc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0134 1.465]. < /ref > < ref > {{harvnb|Wright|1999|p=333}}. < /ref >  In [[Classical Greece]], a small spit or skewer was called an ''obeliskos'' ({{lang|grc|ὀβελίσκος}}). < ref > {{harvnb|Liddell|Scott|1940}}: [https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/cgi-bin/ptext?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.04.0057%3Aentry%3D%2371909 ὀβελίσκος], diminutive of [https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/cgi-bin/ptext?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.04.0057%3Aentry%3D%2371912 ὀβελός] (''obelos''). < /ref >  [[Aristophanes]] mentions such small skewers being used to cook birds. < ref > Aristophanes. ''Acharnians'', [https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Aristoph.+Ach.+1007 & fromdoc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0240 1007]. < /ref > 
 
 In the [[Byzantine Empire]], the Greek author of the ''Prodromic Poems'' (4.231) mentions  " the hot meat shops "  of [[Constantinople]] giving customers slices of meat cooked on spits like souvlaki called ''{{transl|grc|psenasis souglitarea}}''. < ref > {{harvnb|Dalby|2010|pp=70, 225}}. < /ref > 
 
 In [[modern]] times, the [[French people|French]] traveler [[Gustave Flaubert]] saw Greeks  " grilling pieces of meat on a bamboo stick "  when he visited the Greek [[countryside]] of Boeotia in 1850. < ref name= " Street Foods " > {{harvnb|Matalas|Yannakoulia|2000|loc=p. 6 including footnote 8}}. < /ref >  But modern souvlaki was not widespread in Greece until after [[World War II]]. < ref name= " Street Foods " / >  Souvlaki skewers served as fast food began to be sold widely in the 1960s after their introduction by sellers from Boeotia. < ref name= " Street Foods " / >  The word ''souvlaki'' in the [[English language]] was first used in 1942. < ref name= " Webster " > {{cite web|title=Souvlaki|website=[[Merriam-Webster]]|access-date=5 February 2020|url=https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/souvlaki}} < /ref >
