 The Actinobacteria, or Actinomycetota are a group of [[Gram-positive]] [[Bacterium|bacteria]]. They grow on land or in water. < ref name= " Evidence " > {{cite journal |last1=Servin |first1=J. A. |last2=Herbold |first2=C. W. |last3=Skophammer |first3=R. G. |last4=Lake |first4=J. A. |title=Evidence Excluding the Root of the Tree of Life from the Actinobacteria |journal=Molecular Biology and Evolution |date=13 November 2007 |volume=25 |issue=1 |pages=1–4 |doi=10.1093/molbev/msm249 |pmid=18003601 |url=https://academic.oup.com/mbe/article/25/1/1/1098539 |accessdate=2022-07-21}} < /ref >  They include some of the most common life forms in soil, playing important roles in the [[decomposition]] of organic matter and [[humus]] formation. Fungi also play the same role, so some Actinobacteria like Streptomyces were originally classified as fungi. Actinobacteria colonies form branching threads called mycelia. This feature was another reason for the wrongful classification of the actinomycetes, for example, as fungi.  
 
 Some species live in a mutual resource exchange with the roots of plants, such as Frankia, while other species can cause disease, such as the genus Mycobacterium and the bacteria ''[[Mycobacterium tuberculosis]].''
