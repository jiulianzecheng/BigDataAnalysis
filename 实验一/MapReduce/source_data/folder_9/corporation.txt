 A '''corporation''' is a group of [[natural person|person]]s that sit together to make and run a [[business]]. This business is considered an entirely different human and not related to the group which manages it. < ref > {{Cite journal|last=Hirst|first=Scott|date=2018-07-01|title=The Case for Investor Ordering|url=https://scholarship.law.bu.edu/faculty_scholarship/343|journal=The Harvard Law School Program on Corporate Governance Discussion Paper|volume=No. 2017-13}} < /ref >   
 
 Because it is recognized by governments as a separate person entirely, the corporation must pay its own taxes and conform to state and federal law. This difference of persons and corporation gives it special powers. < ref > {{Cite web|title=Types Of Corporations {{!}} Incorporate A Business|url=https://www.corpnet.com/types-of-corporations/#gfp|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171015152228/https://www.corpnet.com/types-of-corporations/#gfp|archive-date=2017-10-15|access-date=2017-06-10|website=www.corpnet.com|language=en}} < /ref >  A corporation's special powers are determined by the law of the country in which the corporation is set up. 
 
 [[Investor]]s(people who own a small part of the company) and [[entrepreneur]]s(The people who founded the company and its ideas) often form [[joint stock company|joint stock companies]]. Therefore, the term ''corporation'' often means such ''business corporations''. Corporations may also be formed for [[local government]] ([[municipal corporation]]), political, religious, and charitable purposes ([[Non-profit organization|not-for-profit corporation]]), or government programs ([[government-owned corporation]]).  [[Condominiums]] are a common kind of non-profit corporation. < ref > {{Cite journal|last=Sim|first=Michael|date=2018|title=Limited Liability and the Known Unknown|url=https://www.ssrn.com/abstract=3121519|journal=Duke Law Journal|volume=68|pages=275â€“332|doi=10.2139/ssrn.3121519|issn=1556-5068|via=SSRN|s2cid=44186028}} < /ref > < ref > {{Cite journal|last1=Hansmann|first1=Henry|last2=Kraakman|first2=Reinier|date=May 1991|title=Toward Unlimited Shareholder Liability for Corporate Torts|url=http://dx.doi.org/10.2307/796812|journal=The Yale Law Journal|volume=100|issue=7|pages=1879|doi=10.2307/796812|issn=0044-0094|jstor=796812}} < /ref > 
 
 In common speech, the word  " corporation "  refers usually to '''limited responsibility corporation'''. That is a business firm where each of the partners invest a sum of money in as the capital of the corporation. They receive [[share (finance)|share]]s for the sum they have invested. If the company becomes [[Bankruptcy|bankrupt]], the business partners are responsible for only the name value of their shares of the company's debts. The partners do not use their personal money to pay for the [[debt]].
