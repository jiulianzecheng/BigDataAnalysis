 An '''ambush''', or a '''surprise attack''', < ref > ''Harrap's essential English Dictionary'', eds. Elaine Higgleton; Anne Seaton (New Delhi: Allied Chambers (India), Ltd., 1996), p. 32 < /ref >  is a long-established [[military tactics|military tactic]]. It is a surprise [[attack]] from a hidden position against an enemy. < ref name=BaillACG > {{cite web |url=http://www.armchairgeneral.com/tactics-101-088-the-ambush-part-1-fundamentals.htm |title=Tactics 101 088: The Ambush, Part 1 – Fundamentals |author=Rick Baillergeon; John Sutherland |publisher=Armchair General L.L.C. |access-date=24 October 2015}} < /ref >  The enemy may be moving or have stopped. An ambush uses the element of surprise to confuse the enemy soldiers long enough to be successful.  
 
 Unlike other surprise attacks, the point of an ambush is not to gain territory. < ref name= " BaillACG "  / >  Instead, it may be used to [[harass]] an enemy. It may also be used to [[Wikt:capture|capture]] or [[Wikt:destroy|destroy]] key [[wikt:asset|asset]]s (people, equipment, supplies, etc.). < ref name= " BaillACG "  / > 
 
 == In history == 
 One of the earliest recorded use of the ambush was in 9 [[AD]] in the [[Battle of the Teutoburg Forest]]. The [[Germanic tribes|Germanic]] leader [[Arminius]] ambushed three [[Roman legion]]s thaï were under the command of [[Varus]]. The 20,000 Roman soldiers were strung out in a long line of march through the densely-wooded [[forest]]. < ref name=JohnsonDM > {{cite web |url=http://www.dailymail.co.uk/home/moslive/article-1267536/Ten-greatest-battlefield-tactics-Rob-Johnson.html |title=Ten of the greatest battlefield tactics |author=Rob Johnson |date=24 April 2010  |publisher=Daily Mail|access-date=24 October 2015}} < /ref >  The Romans fought for days but in the end were completely destroyed. < ref name=JohnsonDM/ > 
 
 The Germanics had not only a military victory but also a [[psychological]] victory over the Romans. < ref name=JohnsonDM/ >
