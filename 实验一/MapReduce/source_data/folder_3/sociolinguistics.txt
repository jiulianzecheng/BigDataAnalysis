'''Sociolinguistics''' is the study of how [[language]] is related to [[society]]. It also studies how people affect language usage. Sociolinguistics studies how variety in language changes between groups of people because of things such as [[race (sociology)|race]], [[gender]], [[Social status|status]], [[Ageing|age]], etc. 
 
 Indian and Japanese linguists first studied the social parts of language in the 1930s. Louis Gauchat in Switzerland also studied this in the early 1900s. However, none of them were known in the West until much later. The study of why [[:en:Language change|language change]] < nowiki/ > s started during the late 1900's in the wave model. [[:en:Thomas Callan Hodson|Thomas Callan Hodson]] was the first person to us the word ''sociolinguistics''. It was in an article he wrote in 1939 called  " Sociolinguistics in India " .  == Applications of sociolinguistics == 
 For example, a sociolinguist might research how a particular variety of language ([[:en:Vernacular|vernacular]]) would not be considered correct in a business place. Sociolinguists might also study the [[:en:Grammar|grammar]], [[:en:Phonetics|phonetics]], and [[:en:Vocabulary|vocabulary]] of this sociolect. Similarly, [[:en:Dialectology|dialectologists]] would study the same things for a [[:en:Regional dialect|regional dialect]]. 
 
 The study of language [[:en:Variation (linguistics)|variation]] focuses on how language changes with social pressure and when in a specific [[:en:Social environment|environment]]. [[:en:Code-switching|Code-switching]] is a word used to explain the language changes in new environments. 
 
 William Labov is often thought of as the most important person who studies sociolinguistics. He is especially known for introducing a way to measure language variation and change. < ref > Paolillo, John C. ''Analyzing Linguistic Variation: Statistical Models and Methods'' CSLI Press 2001, Tagliamonte, Sali ''Analysing Sociolinguistic Variation'' Cambridge, 2006 < /ref >  This made the study of language into a scientific area of study.
