A '''compendium''' (plural: compendia) is a [[wikt:collection|collection]] of [[wikt:information|information]] on a particular [[wikt:topic|topic]]. The amount of information is usually short but detailed. A compendium will often [[Summary|summarize]] a larger work, putting it into fewer words. It is usually not intended to be read from beginning to end. Instead a compendium is used to look up facts. It is very often called a [[:en:wikt:reference work|reference work]] 
 
 The word compendium comes from the [[Latin language|Latin]] word  " compenso " . It means  " to weigh together or balance " . The [[Internet]] has large numbers of compendia in the form of [[database]]s. [[Search engine|Searching]] a database make it very easy to find facts and information. This is causing concern that print compendia (in the form of [[book]]s) may eventually go away. < ref name=Regier95,96 > Willis Goth Regier, ''Quotology'' (Lincoln: University of Nebraska Press, 2010), pp. 95–96 < /ref > 
 
 == Examples == 
 
 One example would be the ''Compendium of the [[Catechism of the Catholic Church]]''. It is organized into 598 questions and answers which summarize the teachings of the [[Catholic Church|Catholic Faith]]. < ref > Catholic Church; United States Conference of Catholic Bishops,''Compendium of the Catechism of the Catholic Church'' (Washington, DC: United States Conference of Catholic Bishops, 2006), pp. xi–xii < /ref > 
 
 The [[Bible]] is another example of a compendium. < ref > ''Reading the Hebrew Bible for a New Millennium'', Volume 1, ed. Wonil Kim (Harrisburg, PA: Trinity Press International, 2000), p. 24 < /ref >  It is a group of many books put together to form the [[Old Testament]] and the [[New Testament]]. 
 
 [[Bartlett's Familiar Quotations]] is a compendium of [[Wisdom|wise]] [[saying]]s by famous people. < ref > Gary Saul Morson, ''The Words of Others: From Quotations to Culture'' (New Haven, CT: Yale University Press, 2011), p. 28 < /ref >
