 A '''lenticel''' is  porous tissue in the [[bark]] of trees. Its cells make large intercellular spaces in the [[periderm]] of the bark. They are found in the woody [[plant stem|stem]]s and [[roots]] of [[dicotyledon]]ous flowering plants. < ref > {{cite web|url=http://www.botgard.ucla.edu/html/botanytextbooks/generalbotany/barkfeatures/fulltextonly.html|first=Arthur C.|last=Gibson|title=''Bark Features'' in General Botany|access-date=2012-08-19|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20131117053401/http://www.botgard.ucla.edu/html/botanytextbooks/generalbotany/barkfeatures/fulltextonly.html|archive-date=2013-11-17|url-status=dead}} < /ref >   
 
 Lenticels are also found in other woody plants, starting in the [[Carboniferous|Carboniferous period]]. The development and increase in these primitive lenticels gave a system  for aeration and [[gas exchange]] in these plants. < ref > {{cite journal|last1=Hook|first1=Donald D.|title=Aeration in Trees|journal=Botanical Gazette|date=December 1972|volume=133|issue=4|pages=443–454|jstor=2474119|doi=10.1086/336669|s2cid=83842497}} < /ref > 
 
 Lenticels work as pores for the direct [[gas exchange|exchange of gases]]. Otherwise the bark is impermeable to gases.
