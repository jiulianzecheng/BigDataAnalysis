 '''Brevicipitidae''' or '''rain frogs''' are a [[Family (biology)|family]] of [[frog]] [[species]]. They live in eastern and southern [[Africa]]. As of 2020, scientists say there are 37 species in 5 genera in Brevicipitidae. < ref name= " AW " > {{aut|AmphibiaWeb: Information on amphibian biology and conservation.}} [web application]. 2013. Version 5.6 (9 January 2013) | author = Frost, Darrel R. | year = 2013 | accessdate = 30 November 2013}} < /ref >  Scientists used to think of Brevicipitidae as a subfamily in the family [[Microhylidae]] (narrow-mouth frogs). By studying [[phylogenetics|phylogenetics]], the scientists changed their minds. Now they think brevicipitine frogs are their own family.  Most adult brevicipitine frogs are not easy to see.  They dig into the soil or dead leaves. Sometimes, some of these frogs climb trees. < ref name= " Müller " / >  In most of these frogs, the adult female frog is [[sexual size dimorphism|much bigger]] than the adult male frog. < ref name= " AW " / > 
 
 The frogs in ''Breviceps'' and ''Probreviceps'' do [[direct development]]. That means their eggs hatch into in small frogs.  They are never [[tadpole]]s. When it is time for the male frog to fertilize the female frog's eggs, frogs in ''Breviceps'' ''glue'' themselves together using sticky chemicals from the [[holocrine glands]] on their bellies and backs. < ref name=  " J. Visser " > {{Cite journal | last1 = Visser | first1 = J. |last2 = Cei | first2 = J.M. | last3 = Gutierrez | first3 = L.S. | title = The histology of dermal glands of mating Breviceps with comments on their possible functional value in microhylids (Amphibia: Anura) | journal = African Zoology | volume = 17 | issue = 1 | year = 1982 | url= https://www.ajol.info/index.php/az/article/view/152307}} < /ref >  The frogs lay small [[clutch (eggs)|clutch]]es of 13–56 eggs (4–8 & nbsp;mm diameter not including the protective capsule). Sometimes, the frog digs a [[burrow]], or hole, for the eggs.
