'''Qualia''' < ref > singular form: '''quale''' < /ref >  is a term [[philosophers]] use for bits of [[perception]] or [[wikt:subjective|subjective]], [[consciousness|conscious experience]]. It is in the field called the philosophy of [[psychology]], or [[philosophy of mind]]. < ref > [http://consc.net/online1.html#qualia Online papers on qualia] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070319191131/http://consc.net/online1.html#qualia |date=2007-03-19 }}, by various authors, compiled by David Chalmers < /ref > 
 
 Examples of qualia include the [[pain]] of a [[headache]], the [[taste]] of [[wine]], or the perceived [[red]] < nowiki/ > ness of an evening [[sky]]. It is a separate bit of [[experience]], different from [[Thought|thinking]] about [[proposition]]s or [[stream of consciousness]] thought. < ref > {{Cite book|title = Current controversies in philosophy of mind |last = Kriegel, Uriah 2014.|publisher = Taylor  &  Francis |isbn = 978-0-415-53086-6|location = New York|pages = 201}} < /ref > 
 
 [[Daniel Dennett]] (b. 1942), American [[philosopher]] and [[cognition|cognitive]] [[scientist]], says ''qualia'' is  " an unfamiliar term for something that could not be more familiar to each of us: the ways things seem to us " . < ref > {{cite web|url=http://ase.tufts.edu/cogstud/papers/quinqual.htm |title=Dennett D. ' & #39;Quining Qualia' & #39; |publisher=Ase.tufts.edu |date=1985-11-21 |access-date=2010-12-03}} < /ref >
