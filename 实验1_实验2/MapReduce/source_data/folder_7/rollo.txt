 '''Rollo''' ({{circa|846}}–{{circa|928}}), also called '''Rolf''' ('''''Hrolf''''') was a [[Viking]] leader.{{efn|While he is sometimes titled [[Duke of Normandy]] this is a title of [[:wikt:convenience|convenience]] used by [[historians]]. The first leaders of Normandy used the title [[count]] or [[marquis]]. It was not until the middle of the eleventh century until the counts of Normandy called themselves Dukes. < ref > François Neveux, ''The Normans; The Conquests that Changed the Face of Europe'', trans. Howard Curtis (London: Constable  &  Robinson Ltd., 2008), p. 69 < /ref >  Flodoard of Reims used the term [[:wikt:princeps|princeps]] when writing about Rollo or his son William I Longsword. < ref name=Flodoardxxi/ >  Titles were sometimes assumed in the [[Carolingian Empire]]. Counts of Anjou or later dukes of Normandy at times assumed their titles without [[royal]] approval. < ref > Maurice Powicke, ''The loss of Normandy: 1189 - 1204; studies in the history of the Angevin Empire'' (Manchester: Manchester University Press, 1999). p. 13 < /ref > }} In 911 he was given lands around [[Rouen]] by the Frankish king [[Charles the Simple]]. He passed on his ever growing [[fief]] to his descendants, known as the [[count]]s and later [[Duke of Normandy|dukes of Normandy]]. He was the first of the dynasty known as the [[House of Normandy]]. 
 
 == Early career == 
 
 Rollo might have been a son of Rognvald, earl of More (in present day [[Norway]]) and was a Viking [[raid]]er in [[France]]. < ref name=Douglas16 >  David C. Douglas, ''William the Conqueror; The Norman Impact upon England'' (Berkeley; Los Angeles, University of California Press, 1964), p. 16 < /ref >  He probably also raided [[Scotland]] and [[Ireland]]. < ref name=Douglas16/ >  Rollo played a part in the [[Siege of Paris (885–86)]] and then captured [[Bayeux]] according to [[Dudo of Saint-Quentin]]. < ref >  François Neveux, ''The Normans; The Conquests that Changed the Face of Europe'', trans. Howard Curtis (London: Constable  &  Robinson Ltd., 2008), p. 59 < /ref >  While Rollo was given lands around Rouen in 911 by the French king, Flodoard of Reims says that there were many bands of [[:wikt:Northmen|Northmen]] raiding in western France at the time. < ref name=Flodoardxxxxi > ''The Annals of Flodoard of Reims 919–966'', ed.  &  trans. Steven Fanning; Bernard S. Bachrach (Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 2011), pp. xx-xxi < /ref >  In his writing he paid more attention to another group of Vikings under a Ragenold. Flodoard had no way of knowing then that of all the Viking bands those under Rollo would grow to become the great [[duchy]] of [[Normandy]]. < ref name=Flodoardxxxxi/ > 
 
 == Beginnings of Normandy == 
 
 In 918 King Charles and Rollo met on the [[border]] of the Vexin and Roumois. The Roumois was the name then for the [[province]] of which Rouen was the capital. < ref name=Crouch4 > David Crouch, ''The Normans; The History of a Dynasty'' (London; New York: Hambledon Continuum, 2007), p. 4 < /ref >  In a charter the king [[confirmed]] to Rollo the city of Rouen. He also gave him all the [[territory]] west to the border of [[Brittany]]. < ref name=Crouch4/ >  By 924 Normandy was growing into a [[principality]]. < ref name=Crouch7 > David Crouch, ''The Normans; The History of a Dynasty'' (London; New York: Hambledon Continuum, 2007), p. 7 < /ref >  It was at this time he acquired [[Bayeux]] and also [[Maine (province)|Maine]]. < ref > ''The Normans in Europe'', trans. Elisabeth van Houts (Manchester; New York: Manchester University Press, 2000), p. 16 < /ref >  Rollo appears in annals only here and there. < ref name=Flodoardxxi > ''The Annals of Flodoard of Reims 919–966'', ed.  &  trans. Steven Fanning; Bernard S. Bachrach (Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 2011), p. xxi < /ref >  He was fighting over the castle of Eu against King [[Rudolph of France|Raoul]]. He also came to the aid of King Charles the Simple against [[Herbert II of Vermandois]]. < ref name=Flodoardxxi/ >  Rollo died about 928. < ref name=Houts2 > ''The Normans in Europe'', ed.  &  trans. Elisabeth van Houts (Manchester; New York: Manchester University Press, 2000), p. 2 < /ref >  He was succeeded by his son [[William I, Duke of Normandy|William Longsword]]. < ref name=Houts2/ > 
 
 == Family == 
  [[File:Rollo+Charles.jpg|thumb|{{center|A 14th century picture showing the marriage between Rollo and the king's daughter Gisela}}]] 
 Rollo's first wife was [[Poppa of Bayeux|Poppa]]. < ref name=SchwennickeII79 > Detlev Schwennicke, ''Europaische Stammtafeln: Stammtafeln zur Geschichte der Europaischen Staaten'', Neue Folge, Band II (Marburg, Germany:  J. A. Stargardt, 1984), Tafel 79 < /ref >  Together they had: 
 
 * William I, Longsword. He succeeded his father as [[:wikt:princeps|princeps]] of Normandy. < ref name=SchwennickeII79/ > 
 * Gerloc, wife of William III, Duke of Aquitaine. < ref name=SchwennickeII79/ >   
 
 Rollo's second wife was Gisela, daughter of Charles III the Simple, King of France.  < ref name=SchwennickeII1 > Detlev Schwennicke, ''Europaische Stammtafeln: Stammtafeln zur Geschichte der Europaischen Staaten'', Neue Folge, Band II (Marburg, Germany:  J. A. Stargardt, 1984), Tafel 1 < /ref >  They had no children. < ref name=SchwennickeII1/ >
