 The '''wrasses''' are a [[family (biology)|family]], the '''Labridae''', of marine [[fish]], many of which are brightly colored. The family is large and diverse, with over 600 [[species]] in 82 [[genera]]. < ref name=Cowman2009 >

 They are usually small fish, mostly less than {{convert|20|cm|in}} long, although the largest, the [[humphead wrasse]], can measure up to {{convert|2.5|m|ft}}. 
 
 == Etymology == 
 The word  " wrasse "  comes via [[Cornish language|Cornish]] from the [[Welsh language|Welsh]] word ''gwrach'' meaning an old woman or hag. < ref > http://dictionary.reference.com/browse/Wrasse < /ref >

 == The cleaner wrasse == 
 ''Main article: [[Cleaner fish]]'' 
 
 '''Cleaner wrasses''' are the best-known of the [[cleaner fish]]. 
 
 == Feeding methods == 
 They are [[carnivore]]s, feeding on a wide range of small [[invertebrate]]s. Many smaller wrasses follow the feeding trails of larger fish, eating invertebrates left by big fish. < ref name=EoF > {{cite book |editor=Paxton, J.R.  &  Eschmeyer, W.N.|author= Choat, J.H.  &  Bellwood, D.R.|year=1998|title=Encyclopedia of Fishes|url=https://archive.org/details/encyclopediaoffi00unse|publisher= Academic Press|location=San Diego|pages= [https://archive.org/details/encyclopediaoffi00unse/page/211 211]|isbn= 0-12-547665-5}} < /ref > 
 
 Wrasses can put their jaws forwards, usually with separate jaw teeth that jut outwards. < ref name= " wainwright " >

 Many species can be recognized by their thick lips. The inside of these lips is curiously folded, which gave rise the German name of ''Lippfische''. The [[dorsal fin]] has 8–21 spines and 6–21 soft rays, usually running most of the length of the back. 
 
 Wrasse are [[sexually dimorphic]]. Many species are capable of changing sex. Juveniles are a mix of males and females (known as Initial Phase or IP individuals) but the largest adults become territory-holding (Terminal Phase or TP) males. < ref name= " wainwright " / >
