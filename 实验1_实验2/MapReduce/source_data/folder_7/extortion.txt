 In [[law]], '''extortion''' is a [[crime|criminal offense]]. It is obtaining [[money]], [[property]], or [[service (economics)|service]] from a [[person]] or [[institution]], through [[coercion]]. This may be by [[threat]] to a [[victim]], to their [[property]] or [[family]]. It may also be by [[false]] [[wikt:claim|claim]] (such as claiming to be a [[Official|public official]]). < ref name=NOLO > {{cite web |url=http://www.nolo.com/dictionary/extortion-term.html |title=Extortion |publisher=NOLO |access-date=8 December 2015}} < /ref >  Another form of extortion is offering a [[business]] [[owner]]  " protection " ; for example, to keep the business free from [[vandalism]] or [[burglary]]. < ref name=FindLaw > {{cite web |url=http://criminal.findlaw.com/criminal-charges/extortion.html |title=Extortion |publisher=FindLaw |access-date=8 December 2015}} < /ref >  In all [[states of the United States]] extortion is a [[felony]]. < ref name=FindLaw/ > 
 
 Extortion may be in person, by [[telephone]], [[mail]], [[e-mail]], [[text message]] or any form of [[Wireless|wireless communication]]. If the extortion involves any form of [[interstate commerce]], it may be a [[Federal government of the United States|federal]] [[crime]]. < ref name=FindLaw/ >  It may involve the threat of [[hacking]]. < ref > {{cite web |url=http://www.secureworks.com/cyber-threat-intelligence/threats/extortion/ |title=New extortion scam aimed at banks in the European Union |publisher=Dell SecureWorks, Inc |access-date=8 December 2015 |archive-date=16 May 2013 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130516095100/http://www.secureworks.com/cyber-threat-intelligence/threats/extortion/ |url-status=dead }} < /ref > 
 
 Extortion, like [[robbery]] and [[blackmail]], are crimes that involve obtaining property by [[illegal]] means. < ref > {{cite web |url=http://www.minicklaw.com/robbery-extortion-and-blackmail-offenses/ |title=Robbery, Extortion, and Blackmail Offenses |author=James Minick |date=14 June 2013 |publisher=Minik Law |access-date=8 December 2015}} < /ref >  The most serious of the three is robbery because it usually involves [[violence]] against the victim. Blackmail is a form of extortion. The threat is usually making [[wikt:damaging|damaging]] or [[Embarrassment|embarrassing]] information public if demands are not met. < ref name=FindLaw/ >  The legal [[definition]] of extortion varies from one [[jurisdiction]] to another. In some states, extortion is only charged against public officials while the same act committed by a private individual is charged as blackmail. < ref > {{cite web |url=http://www.hg.org/extortion.html |title=Extortion and Blackmail Law |publisher=HG.org |access-date=8 December 2015}} < /ref >
