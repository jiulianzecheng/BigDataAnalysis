 The '''gymnosperms''' are a group of [[seed plant]]s which includes [[conifer]]s, [[cycad]]s, ''[[Ginkgo]]'' and [[Gnetophyta|Gnetales]]. < ref > The term comes from the [[Greek language|Greek]] word (γυμνός) for  " naked seed " , ''gymnospermos''. < /ref > 
 
 They have naked seeds, in contrast to the seeds or ovules of [[flowering plant]]s ([[angiosperm]]s) which are enclosed during pollination. Gymnosperm seeds develop either on the surface of scale- or leaf-like appendages of [[Conifer cone|cones]], or at the end of short stalks (''Ginkgo''). < ref > Anderson, Anderson  &  Cleal 2007. Brief history of the gymnosperms: classification, biodiversity, phytogeography and ecology. Strelitzia. '''20''' SANBI. p280. ISBN 978-1-919976-39-6 < /ref > 
 
 The gymnosperms and angiosperms together make up the [[spermatophyte]]s or [[seed plants]]. By far the largest group of living gymnosperms are the [[conifer]]s ([[pine]]s, [[cypress]]es, and relatives), followed by cycads, Gnetales (''[[Gnetum]]'', ''[[Ephedra (genus)|Ephedra]]'' and ''[[Welwitschia]]''), and ''[[Ginkgo]]'' (a single living species). < ref > {{Cite journal|date=2018-09-01|title=Sexual systems in gymnosperms: A review|url=https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1439179117304498|journal=Basic and Applied Ecology|language=en|volume=31|pages=1–9|doi=10.1016/j.baae.2018.05.009|issn=1439-1791|last1=Walas|first1=Łukasz|last2=Mandryk|first2=Wojciech|last3=Thomas|first3=Peter A.|last4=Tyrała-Wierucka|first4=Żanna|last5=Iszkuło|first5=Grzegorz|s2cid=90740232}} < /ref > 
 
 [[Fossil]] gymnosperms include many that do not belong to the four modern groups, including the so-called  " seed ferns "  ([[Pteridospermatophyta|Pteridosperms]]) and the  " cycadeoids "  ([[Bennettitales]]). Most of the Gymnosperms became [[extinct]] in the [[Cenozoic|Cenozoic era]] (from 65 million years ago to present day). < ref > {{cite journal |author=Crisp, M.D.; Cook, L.G. |year=2011 |title=Cenozoic extinctions account for the low diversity of extant gymnosperms compared with angiosperms |journal=New Phytologist |volume=192 |issue=4 |pages=997–1009 |doi=10.1111/j.1469-8137.2011.03862.x|pmid=21895664 |s2cid=23823843 }} < /ref > 
 
 Some genera have [[mycorrhiza]], fungal associations with roots (''Pinus''). In some others (''Cycas'') small specialised roots have  nitrogen-fixing [[cyanobacteria]].
