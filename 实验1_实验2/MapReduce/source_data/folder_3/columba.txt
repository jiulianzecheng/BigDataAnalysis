Edmonds|publisher=The Catholic Encyclopedia |access-date=12 January 2016}} < /ref > ) was an [[Irish people|Irish]] [[priest]] and [[missionary]]. < ref name=MedSourceBook > {{cite web |url=http://legacy.fordham.edu/halsall/basis/columba-e.asp |title=Medieval Sourcebook: Adamnan: Life of St. Columba, Introduction [Seth Seyfried] |publisher=Fordham University |access-date=12 January 2016 |archive-date=13 March 2016 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160313055807/http://legacy.fordham.edu/halsall/basis/columba-e.asp |url-status=dead }} < /ref >  He left [[Ireland]] in 563 AD on his mission to bring [[Christianity]] to [[Dál Riata]], now [[west]]ern [[Scotland]]. < ref name=BBC > {{cite web |url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/history/scottishhistory/earlychurch/features_earlychurch_iona.shtml |title=St Columba (Colum Cille) |publisher=BBC |access-date=12 January 2016}} < /ref >  He built the famous [[monastery]] on the [[Iona|Island of Iona]] which became known as a  " holy island'. < ref name=BBC/ > 
 
 == Early life == 
 Columba was born on December 7, 521 A.D. in Gartan, [[County Donegal]]. < ref name=MedSourceBook/ >  His father was Fedhlimidh, a [[Grandparent#Great-grandparents|great-grandson]] of [[Niall of the Nine Hostages]], [[4th century]] [[High King of Ireland]]. < ref name=UJA3 > 'Saint Columba ', ''Ulster Journal of Archaeology'', First Series, Vol. 6 (1858), p. 3 < /ref >  His mother was Eithne of the [[Uí Néill]] [[clan]]. < ref name=MedSourceBook/ >  She was a member of the [[royal family]] of [[Leinster]]. < ref name=UJA3/ >  While still young he decided on a [[Monastery|monastic life]]. He was educated in Ireland at schools in [[Moville]], [[Glasnevin]] and [[Clonard Abbey]]. < ref name=Crooks621 > George R Crooks; John Fletcher Hurst, ''Library of biblical and theological literature'', Volume VII (New York: Eaton  &  Mains, 1900), pp. 621–22 < /ref >  He [[wikt:found|found]]ed the Irish monasteries at [[Derry]] and at [[Durrow, County Offaly]]. < ref name=Crooks621/ > 
 
 == Iona == 
 In about 563 [[AD]], Columba and his [[wikt:follower|follower]]s left Donegal by boat and traveled to the [[island]] of Iona off the [[west]] [[coast]] of Scotland. < ref name=Bradley15 > Ian Bradley, ''Columba: Pilgrim and Penitent'' (Glasgow: Wild Goose Publications, 1996), p. 15 < /ref >  His mission to Scotland may have been more a case of [[penance]] for a [[battle]] he may have caused. < ref name=JohnsonHUK > {{cite web |url=http://www.historic-uk.com/HistoryUK/HistoryofScotland/St-Columba-the-Isle-of-Iona/ |title=St Columba and the Isle of Iona |author=Ben Johnson |publisher=Historic UK |access-date=12 January 2016}} < /ref >  He refused to return a copy of the [[Gospels]] he had copied without permission. He upset the king of Ireland by his refusal, which led to a battle won by his clan. < ref name=JohnsonHUK/ >  Columba felt remorse over the deaths this caused. < ref name=JohnsonHUK/ >  Once there he and his [[monk]]s began the [[religious conversion]] of most of Scotland which at that time was [[pagan]]. < ref name=JohnsonHUK/ >  Their [[wikt:fame|fame]] and [[influence]] spread to most of [[Europe]]. It became a place of [[pilgrimage]]. It also became known as a holy island where 48 kings of Scotland, 4 kings of Ireland and 8 kings of [[Norway]] are buried. < ref name=JohnsonHUK/ >  In 597 Comumba died on Iona. < ref name=Wallace39 > Martin Wallace, ''Celtic Saints'' (San Francisco, CA: Chronicle Books, 1995), p. 39 < /ref >  Books on his life began being written a [[century]] later. The best known of these was written by [[Adomnán]] sometime between 697 and 700. He was proclaimed a saint before the modern process of [[canonization]].
