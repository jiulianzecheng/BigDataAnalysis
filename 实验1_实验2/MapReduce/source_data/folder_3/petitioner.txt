 A '''petitioner''' is a very specific [[legal]] [[Terminology|term]]. It is the [[person]] who presents a formal [[legal]] [[petition]] to a [[court]] or a [[legislature]] that requests a certain action be taken. < ref name=Farlex > {{cite web |url=http://legal-dictionary.thefreedictionary.com/petitioner |title=Petitioner |website=The Free Dictionary |publisher=Farlex |access-date=17 April 2016}} < /ref >  A petitioner is also the person who files a [[motion (legal)|motion ]] or an [[appeal]] to a higher [[court]]. < ref > {{cite web |url=http://www.justanswer.com/criminal-law/4tc9m-does-mean-petitioner-court-ca.html |title=Petitioner |publisher=JustAnswer LLC |access-date=17 April 2016 }}{{Dead link|date=August 2021 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }} < /ref >  A party who petitions the [[Supreme Court of the United States|Supreme Court]] to review a [[Legal case|case]] is known as either the petitioner or the appellant. < ref > {{cite web |url=http://scdb.wustl.edu/documentation.php?var=petitioner |title=Petitioner |publisher=The Supreme Court Database |access-date=17 April 2016}} < /ref >  The person or [[wikt:entity|entity]] (such as a [[corporation]] or [[government]]) who opposes the petition is called a [[respondent]]. < ref name=Farlex/ >  In some cases a third party, an ''[[amicus curiae]]'' (friend of the court), may be heard. 
 
 == Appeal process == 
 In the case of an appeal, the petitioner is usually the party who lost in a lower court. This can be either the [[plaintiff]] or the [[defendant]] who files the petition to the higher court for [[judicial review]]. < ref > {{cite web |url=https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/petitioner |title=Petitioner |publisher=Legal Information Institute, Cornell University Law School |access-date=17 April 2016}} < /ref >  If the petition is granted, the petitioner files an [[Brief (law)|appellate brief]] giving the legal arguments for filing the appeal. < ref name=LL > {{cite web|url=http://www.rotlaw.com/legal-library/what-is-a-petitioner/|title=What is a petitioner?|publisher=Legal Library/Rottenstein Law Group LLP|access-date=17 April 2016|archive-date=5 April 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160405213056/http://www.rotlaw.com/legal-library/what-is-a-petitioner/|url-status=dead}} < /ref >  The respondent is given a short period of time to respond to the brief with arguments for their side of the case and to [[wikt:refute|refute]] any [[argument]]s made by the petitioner. < ref name=LL/ >  In some [[jurisdiction]]s, the petitioner is allowed to file a short [[reply (legal term)|reply]]. In all three cases, arguments must be based on the [[trial]] [[Document|record]] which includes the [[Transcript (law)|Transcript]]s of [[wikt:testimony|testimony]] and [[evidence]] from the original trial. 
 
 == Civil law == 
 In [[civil law (common law)|civil law]] the person who files the case against another person is called the petitioner or plaintiff. < ref name=moSupremeCt > {{cite web |url=https://www.courts.mo.gov/page.jsp?id=3772 |title=Legal Terms |publisher=Your Missouri Courts, Supreme Court of Missouri |access-date=17 April 2016}} < /ref >  In a [[divorce]] this is the party who files or starts the action to legally end the marriage. < ref name=moSupremeCt/ >
