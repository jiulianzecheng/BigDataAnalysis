'''Turpentine''' (spirit of turpentine, oil of turpentine, wood turpentine) or  '''turps''' is a [[solvent]] and a source of materials for organic [[synthesis]]. < ref > {{cite book |title=The artist's handbook of materials and techniques |url=https://archive.org/details/artistshandbooko00maye_0 |last=Mayer |first=Ralph |year=1991 |edition=5th |publisher=Viking |location=New York |isbn=0-670-83701-6 |page=[https://archive.org/details/artistshandbooko00maye_0/page/404 404]}} < /ref > 
 
 Turpentine is a solvent fluid got by [[distillation|distilling]] [[resin]] from live trees, mainly [[pine]]s. It is a complex mixture of [[hydrocarbons]]. < ref name=Kent > Kent, James A. 1983. ''Riegel's handbook of industrial chemistry''. 8th ed, Van Nostrand Reinhold. ISBN 978-0442201647 p.569 < /ref >  As a solvent, turpentine is used for thinning [[oil paint|oil-based paints]], for producing [[varnish]]es, and as a raw material for the chemical industry. 
 
 The word ''turpentine'' comes from the Greek word τερεβινθίνη ''terebinthine'', the name of a species of tree, the [[Pistacia terebinthus|terebinth]] tree. < ref > {{cite book | last=Barnhart  | first=R.K. | title=The Barnhart concise dictionary of etymology | url=https://archive.org/details/barnhartconcised0000unse  | location=New York | publisher=Harper Collins | year=1995 | isbn=0-06-270084-7}} < /ref >   
 
 It is hazardous. It is dangerous to inhale, and can cause fires. Because of this, [[white spirit]] (mineral turps) or other substitutes are often used. 
 They are very different chemically. < ref > Dieter Stoye,  " Solvents "  in ''Ullmann's encyclopedia of industrial chemistry'', 2002, Wiley-VCH, Wienheim. {{doi|10.1002/14356007.a24_437}} < /ref >
