 ''This is an article about the colour mauve. For information on the Flemish painter, see [[Anton Mauve]].'' 
 
 '''Mauve''' (pronounce to rhyme with  " Jove "  and  " stove " ) is a pale [[purple]] [[colour]].  It is similar to [[lavender (colour)|lavender]] and [[lilac (colour)|lilac]].  The name comes from French form of ''malva'' meaning the  " [[mallow]] "  flower.  
 
 Another name for this color is '''mallow'''. 
 
 The first recorded use of ''mallow'' as a colour name in [[English language|English]] was in 1611.  < ref >  Maerz and Paul ''A Dictionary of Color'' New York:1930 McGraw-Hill Page 198; Color Sample of Mallow: Page 125 Plate 51 Color Sample I3  < /ref >   
 
 In 1856, an eighteen year old [[Chemist]] [[William Henry Perkin]] was trying to make artificial [[quinine]]. An unexpected residue caught his eye. It turned out to be the first [[aniline]] [[dye]], [[mauveine]]. 
 {{-}} 
 ==Meaning of mauve==  
 
 * Mauve became very popular in the 1890s.  This decade was sometimes referred to as the ''Mauve Decade'', because [[William Henry Perkin]]'s [[aniline]] dye allowed the widespread use of that colour in [[Fashion (clothing)|fashion]].   
 
 * The colour mauve became associated with [[homosexuality]] because well known figures in the [[art]] world during the 1890s--a [[decade]] that was called the ''Mauve Decade''--were [[gay]] such as [[author]] [[Oscar Wilde]] and [[artist]] [[Aubrey Beardsley]].
