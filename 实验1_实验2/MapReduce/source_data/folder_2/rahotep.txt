'''Rahotep''' (also spelled '''Re hotep''') was an [[Ancient Egypt|ancient Egyptian]] prince of the 4th dynasty. he was the high priest of [[Ra|Re]] in the city of [[Heliopolis (ancient)|Heliopolis]]. He is famous for his statue which was discovered by the Auguste Mariette in 1871 and is today in the [[Egyptian Museum]] in [[Cairo]]. Rahotep, according to some [[Egyptology|Egyptologists]], was the son of King Sneferu,  others believe he was son of [[Huni]].  < ref > {{cite book|last=Hawass|first=Zahi A.|title=Mountains of the Pharaohs: The Untold Story of the Pyramid Builders|year=2006|publisher=Doubleday Books|isbn=978-0-385-50305-1|page=[https://archive.org/details/mountainsofphara00zahi/page/22 22]|url-access=registration|url=https://archive.org/details/mountainsofphara00zahi/page/22}} < /ref >

 ==Life== 
 Rahotep's titles were written on a stunning statue found in Meidum in 1871. The statue, along with one of his wife Nofret, was discovered by Auguste Mariette. Rahotep held the titles of High Priest of [[Ra]] at [[Heliopolis (ancient)|Heliopolis]], Director of Expeditions, Supervisor of Works, and “the son of the king, begotten of his body”. < ref > {{cite book|last=Rice|first=Michael|title=Who's who in Ancient Egypt|year=1999|publisher=Routledge|isbn=978-0-415-15448-2|url=https://archive.org/details/whoswhoinancient0000rice|url-access=registration|quote=Rahotep priest nofret.|page=[https://archive.org/details/whoswhoinancient0000rice/page/164 164]}}| < /ref > < ref > {{cite book|last=El-Shahawy|first=Abeer|title=The Egyptian Museum in Cairo: A Walk Through the Alleys of Ancient Egypt|year=2005|publisher=Dar al-Mushaf|isbn=978-977-17-2183-3|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=cAyjwKyoHiEC & dq=Rahotep+titles & pg=PA71|page=71}} < /ref > 
 
 His older brother was Nefermaat I, and his younger brother was Ranefer. Rahotep passed away at a young age, so his half-brother [[Khufu]] became become [[pharaoh]] after Sneferu’s death. 
 
 Rahotep’s wife was [[Nofret]], and her parents are unknown. They had three sons – [[Djedi]], Itu, and Neferkau – and three daughters – Mereret, Nedjemib, and Sethtet. All of them are depicted in Rahotep’s tomb. < ref > {{Cite book|url=https://archive.org/details/completeroyalfam0000dods_f9x1?view=theater|title=The Complete Royal Families Of Ancient Egypt With Over 300 Illustrations 90 In Color|last=Dodson|first=Aidan|pages=53-61}} < /ref >
