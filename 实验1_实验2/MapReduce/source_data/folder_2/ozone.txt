 '''Ozone''', or '''trioxygen''', is a [[chemical]] with the symbol '''O < sub > 3 < /sub > '''. This means one [[molecule]] of ozone is made of three [[oxygen]] [[atom]]s. Ozone is rarely called ''trioxygen'', even though this is its [[IUPAC]] [[systematic name]]. < ref > {{Cite book|title=Nomenclature of Inorganic Chemistry IUPAC Recommendations 2005|publisher=RSC Publishing|year=2005|isbn=0-85404-438-8|url=http://old.iupac.org/publications/books/rbook/Red_Book_2005.pdf|at=IR-3.4.3|quote=7. Formula ... O < sub > 3 < /sub >  ... Systematic name ... trinitrogen ... Acceptable alternative name ... ozone|access-date=16 June 2020|archive-date=2019-12-19|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191219222154/http://old.iupac.org/publications/books/rbook/Red_Book_2005.pdf}} < /ref >   Ozone is formed from [[oxygen|oxygen gas (O < sub > 2 < /sub > )]] by the action of [[ultraviolet light]] and also [[atmosphere|atmospheric]] [[electrical discharge]]s. It is present in low concentrations throughout the Earth's atmosphere. In total, ozone makes up only 0.6 ppm ([[parts per million]]) of the atmosphere by volume. 
 
 Ozone is important to [[life]] on [[planet]] [[Earth]]. There is a portion of the [[stratosphere]] with a high concentration of ozone, called the [[ozone layer]]. The ozone layer is 10 to 50 kilometers above the ground. It filters out damaging [[ultraviolet]] [[radiation]] from the [[Sun]], < ref > {{cite web|url=https://www.mfe.govt.nz/environmental-reporting/atmosphere/levels-stratospheric-ozone-indicator/report-card-2012.html|title=Stratospheric ozone|publisher=Ministry for the Environment (New Zealand)|access-date=2014-03-03|date=2013-07-18|archive-date=2015-01-14|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150114230929/http://mfe.govt.nz/environmental-reporting/atmosphere/levels-stratospheric-ozone-indicator/report-card-2012.html|url-status=dead}} < /ref >  like a kind of sun screen. Without this ozone layer things would not have been able to live on the surface of our planet. The ozone layer also absorbs a lot of heat from the sun's rays.   
 
 However, ozone is toxic to [[animal]]s and [[plant]]s above concentrations of about 0.1 ppm. In [[human]]s, it can cause nasal and [[throat]] irritation, and [[nausea]]. < ref name= " und " > {{cite web|url=http://www.understandingozone.com/limits.asp|title=Ozone Safety Limits|publisher=Understanding Ozone|access-date=2013-11-21}} < /ref >  Extended exposure can cause [[lung]] [[oedema]]. < ref name=und / >  0.100 ppm is the maximum allowable limit for industrial, public, or occupied spaces in [[England]], [[Japan]], [[France]], the [[Netherlands]] and [[Germany]]. < ref name=und / > 
 
 The ozone near the ground forms because of [[pollution]]. It is formed by a [[chemical reaction]] between [[volatile organic compound]]s and [[nitrogen oxide]]s. These molecules are common pollutants in cities. Ozone at ground level creates [[smog]]. < ref > {{Cite web|date=14 June 2022|title=Ground-level Ozone Basics|url=https://www.epa.gov/ground-level-ozone-pollution/ground-level-ozone-basics|access-date=10 February 2023|website=www.epa.gov}} < /ref >
