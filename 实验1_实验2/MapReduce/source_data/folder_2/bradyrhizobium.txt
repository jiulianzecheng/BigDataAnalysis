 '''''Bradyrhizobium''''' is a genus of [[Gram-negative]] soil [[bacteria]] that naturally occur as free-living soil bacteria or in [[Symbiosis|symbiotic relationships]] with legumes. ''Bradyrhizobium'' is known for [[nitrogen fixation]], which converts atmospheric nitrogen into ammonia (N < sub > 2 < /sub >  - NH < sub > 3 < /sub > ). This process allows [[plant]]s and other organisms to use [[organic compound]]s, such as [[nitrate]]s, more readily. < ref > Wagner, S. C.. (2011) Biological Nitrogen Fixation. ''Nature Education Knowledge'' 3(10):15 < /ref > < ref > Wongdee, J., Boonkerd, N., Teaumroong, N., Tittabutr, P.,  &  Giraud, E. (2018). Regulation of Nitrogen Fixation in ''Bradyrhizobium'' sp. Strain DOA9 Involves Two Distinct NifA Regulatory Proteins That Are Functionally Redundant During Symbiosis but Not During Free-Living Growth. ''Frontiers in microbiology'', ''9'', 1644.  < nowiki > https://doi.org/10.3389/fmicb.2018.01644 < /nowiki > < /ref >  Nitrogen-fixing bacteria such as ''Bradyrhizobium'' are crucial to chemical cycles in the soil. 
 
 == Microbiology == 
 
 === Ecology === 
 ''Bradyrhizobium'' species are Gram-negative, rod-shaped (also called bacilli) with a [[Flagellum|flagella]] (like a tail) to help them move through their environment. Gram-negative bacteria are smaller types of bacteria with thin cell walls that are sensitive to drought or water stress. < ref > {{Cite web|last=Hoorman|first=James J.|date=June 6, 2016|title=Role of Soil Bacteria|url=https://ohioline.osu.edu/factsheet/anr-36|access-date=July 2, 2023|website=The Ohio State University: College of Food, Agriculture, and Environmental Sciences}} < /ref >  They are commonly found in the soil where they can form relationships with plant roots such as [[pea]]s, [[clover]], and other [[legume]]s in a mutually beneficial, or [[Symbiosis|symbiotic]], relationship. In exchange for their nitrogen fixation, the ''Bradyrhizobium'' benefit from [[carbohydrate]]s made by the plant. They are also common in forest soils, although these species tend not to fix nitrogen. < ref > {{Cite journal|last=VanInsberghe|first=David|last2=Maas|first2=Kendra R.|last3=Cardenas|first3=Erick|last4=Strachan|first4=Cameron R.|last5=Hallam|first5=Steven J.|last6=Mohn|first6=William W.|date=November 2015|title=Non-symbiotic Bradyrhizobium ecotypes dominate North American forest soils|url=https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25909973/|journal=The ISME journal|volume=9|issue=11|pages=2435–2441|doi=10.1038/ismej.2015.54|issn=1751-7370|pmc=4611507|pmid=25909973}} < /ref >
