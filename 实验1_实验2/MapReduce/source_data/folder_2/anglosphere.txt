'''Anglosphere''' is a [[term]] used to mean the group of [[English language|English]]-speaking [[nation]]s with a similar [[cultural heritage]].  
 [[Image:Anglospeak (subnational version).svg|thumb|center|500px|The Anglosphere: Places where [[English language|English]] is an [[official language]] and the [[first language]] of most people are in dark blue. Places where English is an official language, but not the first language of most people, are in light blue. ]] 
 
 The Anglosphere includes the [[United Kingdom]], the [[United States]], and the main members of the [[Commonwealth of Nations]] such as [[Canada]] (except the majority [[French language|French]]-speaking province of [[Quebec]] and the majority [[Inuit languages|Inuit]]-speaking territory of [[Nunavut]]), [[Australia]] and [[New Zealand]]. [[Ireland]], a former [[Commonwealth of Nations]] member, is in the Anglosphere.  
 
 The term was invented at the end of the 20th century. 
 
 == Other meanings == 
 The word means more than just those countries where [[English language|English]] is a [[First language|first]] or [[second language]]. To be considered part of the Anglosphere requires sharing basic customs and values.  " Nations comprising the Anglosphere share a common [[History|historical narrative]] in which the [[Magna Carta]], the [[Bill of Rights 1689|English]] and [[United States Bill of Rights|American Bills of Rights]], and such [[Common law]] principles as trial by [[jury]], [[presumption of innocence]] " . < ref name=ReynoldsGuard > {{cite news |title=Explaining the 'Anglosphere' |author=Glenn Reynolds |newspaper=The Guardian |date=28 October 2004 |url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2004/oct/28/uselections2004.usa4 |access-date=16 December 2014}} < /ref >  Since the Great Rapprochement of the early 20th century, the United States and Great Britain were far more friendly than before. Their military alliance during [[World War II]] has lasted ever since.   
 
 By the time of the [[2003 invasion of Iraq|2003 Iraq war]] the [[political]] use of the word meant the cultural tendency towards [[military]] [[wikt:intervention|intervention]], particularly by the [[US]], the [[UK]] and Australia. < ref name=Haglund91 > David G. Haglund; Joseph T. Jockel, 'Anglosphere Introduction', 
 ''International Journal'', Vol. 60, No. 1 (Winter, 2004/2005), p. 91 < /ref >  [[wikt:Strategically|Strategically]], the word has come to mean that the US is usually backed by Britain and Australia, but not so much by Canada. In this sense, Canada is and sometimes is not considered a part of the Anglosphere. This has been since the [[Pierre Trudeau]] era. < ref name=ReynoldsGuard/ >  The United States of America is generally considered by many to be the dominant country in the Anglosphere. 
 
 Some go so far as to call it an Anglospherist [[school of thought]].
