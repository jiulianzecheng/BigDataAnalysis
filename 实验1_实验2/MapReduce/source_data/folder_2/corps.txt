 A '''corps''' is a [[Military organization|military unit]] usually made up of two or more [[division (military)|division]]s. < ref name=corpsDC > {{cite web |url=http://www.dictionary.com/browse/corps |title=corps |publisher=Dictionary.com, LLC |access-date=17 July 2016}} < /ref >  In the [[United States Army]], a corps is the largest tactical unit that can plan and carry out its own [[wikt:mission|mission]]s. < ref > {{cite web |url=http://www.nbcnews.com/id/14903261/ns/us_news-military/t/words-war-understanding-military-jargon/#.V4uV7_krLIU |title=Words of War: Understanding Military Jargon |publisher= NBCNews.com |access-date=17 July 2016}} < /ref >  The term can also be applied to a military unit with a specific function such as a [[Quartermaster Corps (United States Army)|Quartermaster Corps]] or an [[Intelligence Corps (United Kingdom)|Intelligence Corps]]. < ref name=corpsDC/ >  It can be applied to a branch of a military such as the [[United States Marine Corps]]. < ref > {{cite web |url=http://time.com/4104708/marine-corps-history/ |title=How the U.S. Marine Corps Was Founded Twice |author=Merrill Fabry |date=10 November 2015 |publisher=Time Inc |access-date=17 July 2016}} < /ref >  It can also mean a body of [[officer]]s such as a [[Cadet Corps]]. The word has other uses. A group of people engaged in the same work can be called a corps. An [[example]] is the [[Peace Corps]]. The word comes from the [[Old French]] ''cors'' meaning  " body, person, corpse, life " . < ref name=corpsOED > {{cite web |url=http://www.etymonline.com/index.php?term=corps |title=corps (n)|publisher=Online Etymology Dictionary |access-date=17 July 2016}} < /ref >  That word, in turn, is based on the [[Latin]] word ''corpus'' meaning  " body " . < ref name=corpsOED/ >
