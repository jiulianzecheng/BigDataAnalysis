 The '''Odawa''' < ref > {{cite web | url=https://www.firstnationsseeker.ca/Ottawa.html | title=Odawa Ottawa First Nation Portal Websites }} < /ref >  (also '''Ottawa''' or '''Odaawaa''' {{IPAc-en|oʊ|ˈ|d|ɑː|w|ə}}) are an [[Indigenous peoples of the Americas|Indigenous American]] ethnic group. Their name means  " traders " . They are part of the [[Indigenous peoples of the Northeastern Woodlands|Northeastern Woodlands]] cultures. Their language is part of the [[Algonquian languages|Algonquian language family]]. They mostly live in the northern [[United States]] and southern [[Canada]]. The [[Federal government of the United States|US Government]] federally recognizes these [[Native Americans in the United States|Native Americans]] as a tribe. They have numerous recognized [[First Nations]] [[band government|bands]] in Canada. They are one of the [[Anishinaabe]]g. They are related to but different from the [[Ojibwe]] and [[Potawatomi]] peoples. < ref > [https://www.thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/odawa Odawa] at [[The Canadian Encyclopedia]], accessed September 4, 2019 < /ref > 
 
 == History == 
 The Odawa may have come from some [[Hopewell]] traditions. < ref > {{Cite web|url=http://www.ontarioarchaeology.on.ca/summary/middlew.htm|access-date=2022-08-06|title=Archived copy|archive-date=2009-07-15|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090715213157/http://www.ontarioarchaeology.on.ca/summary/middlew.htm|url-status=bot: unknown}} < /ref >  The Odawa were historically grouped with the [[Ojibwe]] and [[Potawatomi]]. The three were allied in the Council of Three Fires. < ref > Williamson, Pamela, and Roberts, John (2nd ed. 2004). ''First Nations Peoples'', p. 102. Toronto: Emond Montgomery Publications. [[ISBN (identifier)|ISBN]] [[Special:BookSources/1-55239-144-2|1-55239-144-2]]. < /ref >  The fought against other tribes like the [[Iroquois]] and [[Dakota people|Dakota]].  
 
 The Odawa were important in the [[fur trade]]. They traded beaver, deer, marten, raccoon, fox, otter, and muskrat. The Odawa got metal tools, cloths, weapons, jewelry and alcohol in return. < ref > The Wisconsin Cartographers' Guild (1998). ''Wisconsin's Past and Present: A Historical Atlas''. Madison, WI: The University of Wisconsin Press. p. 4. < /ref >  The Odawa fought wars over trading. They fought against the powerful [[Mohawk people|Mohawk]] and [[Iroquois]] in the [[Beaver Wars]].  
 
 Many Natives later died from European diseases. The natives were not immune to these new diseases.  
 
 In 1752, the French [[Charles Michel de Langlade|Charles Langlade]] attacked the [[Miami people|Miami]] people at [[Pickawillany]] with Odawa, Potawatomi, and Ojibwe. The Miami surrendered and handed over Englishmen for [[Negotiation|negotiations]]. The Odawa [[Cannibalism|ate]] one Englishman and the Chief of the town. This was one of the events that led to increased tension in the Ohio Country. This eventually led to the [[French and Indian War]] between France and Britain. < ref > {{Cite web|title=Raid On Pickawillany|url=https://worldhistoryproject.org/1752/6/21/raid-on-pickawillany|access-date=2022-08-06|website=World History Project|language=en|archive-date=2022-09-27|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220927111908/https://worldhistoryproject.org/1752/6/21/raid-on-pickawillany|url-status=dead}} < /ref >     
 
 The Odawa were together with the [[French colonial empire|French]] against the [[British Empire|British]] in the [[French and Indian War]] or [[Seven Years' War]] (1756-1763). [[Pontiac (Ottawa leader)|Pontiac]] was an important Odawa chief. Chief [[Egushawa]] allied with the British during the [[American Revolutionary War|American Revolution]] (1775-1783). < ref > Barnes, Celia (2003). ''Native American Power in the United States, 1783–1795'', p. 203. Fairleigh Dickinson University Press. [[ISBN (identifier)|ISBN]] [[Special:BookSources/0-8386-3958-5|0-8386-3958-5]]. < /ref > 
 
 After the American Revolution, Odawa and other tribes fought against the [[United States]] in the [[Northwest Indian War]]. The natives lost this war and had to give up much land The Odawa gave lands in the [[Treaty of Greenville]] (1795). They gave up more land in the [[Treaty of Detroit]] (1807). < ref > {{Cite web|title=Treaty Between the Ottawa, Chippewa, Wyandot, and Potawatomi Indians.|url=https://www.loc.gov/item/2021667584/|access-date=2022-08-06|website=Library of Congress, Washington, D.C. 20540 USA}} < /ref >  After the [[Indian Removal Act|Indian Removal Act of 1830]], the remaining Odawa moved to [[Kansas]]. < ref > {{Cite web|title=Ottawa|url=http://www.tolatsga.org/otta.html|access-date=2022-08-06|website=www.tolatsga.org|archive-date=2016-04-06|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160406224057/http://www.tolatsga.org/otta.html|url-status=dead}} < /ref >
