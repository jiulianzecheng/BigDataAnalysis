 A '''pseudoscorpion''' (or '''book scorpion''') is an [[arachnid]]. They can be {{convert|2|to|8|mm|in}} long. < ref name= " Penn " > [[Pennsylvania State University]], Department: [http://www.ento.psu.edu/extension/factsheets/pseudoscorpion.htm Entomological Notes: Pseudoscorpion Fact Sheet] < /ref >  The largest known species is ''[[Garypus titanius]]'' of [[Ascension Island]] at  {{convert|12|mm}}. < ref name= " Ascension " > {{cite web |publisher=Ascension Island Conservation Centre |url=http://www.ascensionconservation.org.ac/pdf/35-O-Endemic-invertebrates-leaflet.pdf |title=Endemic invertebrates |access-date=2012-06-08 |archive-date=2010-05-09 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100509081231/http://www.ascensionconservation.org.ac/pdf/35-O-Endemic-invertebrates-leaflet.pdf |url-status=dead }} < /ref > < ref name= " agric " > {{cite web |publisher=Agricultural Research Council (South Africa) |url=http://www.arc.agric.za/home.asp?PID=1 & ToolID=63 & ItemID=1769 |title=Pseudoscorpions |access-date=2012-06-08 |archive-date=2012-02-22 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120222035906/http://www.arc.agric.za/home.asp?PID=1 & ToolID=63 & ItemID=1769 |url-status=dead }} < /ref > 
 
 Pseudoscorpions eat [[clothes moth]] [[larvae]], [[carpet beetle]] larvae, [[booklice]], [[ant]]s, [[mite]]s, and small [[fly|flies]]. Because of this, they are liked by humans. The pseudoscorpions are small and cannot harm humans. They are rarely seen because of their size. Some species of pseudoscorpions do a  " mating dance "  to attract mates. < ref > {{cite journal |author=Peter Weygoldt |year=1966 |title=Spermatophore web formation in a pseudoscorpion |journal=Science |volume=153 |issue=3744 |pages=1647–1649 |doi=10.1126/science.153.3744.1647 |pmid=17802636|bibcode=1966Sci...153.1647W |s2cid=32393067 }} < /ref >  The eggs will stay with the mother until the pseudoscorpions are about a month old. < ref > {{cite journal |author=Heather C. Proctor |year=1993 |url=http://www.americanarachnology.org/JoA_free/JoA_v21_n2/JoA_v21_p156.pdf |title=Mating biology resolves trichotomy for cheliferoid pseudoscorpions (Pseudoscorpionida, Cheliferoidea) |journal=Journal of Arachnology |volume=21 |issue=2 |pages=156–158}} < /ref >  There are more than 3,300 species of pseudoscorpions recorded. < ref > {{cite journal |author=William A. Shear, Wolfgang Schawaller  &  Patricia M. Bonamo |year=1989 |title=Record of Palaeozoic pseudoscorpions |journal=Nature |volume=342 |issue=6242 |pages=527–529 |doi=10.1038/341527a0|bibcode=1989Natur.341..527S |s2cid=4324620 }} < /ref > < ref > {{cite journal |author=Wolfgang Schawaller, William A. Shear  &  Patricia M. Bonamo |year=1991 |title=The first Paleozoic pseudoscorpions (Arachnida, Pseudoscorpionida) |journal=American Museum Novitates |issue=3009 }} < /ref >  The oldest known fossil of pseudoscorpions dates back 380 [[million]] years to the [[Devonian]] period.
