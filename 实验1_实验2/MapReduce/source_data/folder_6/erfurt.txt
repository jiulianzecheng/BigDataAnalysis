 '''Erfurt''' is the [[capital city]] of the [[States of Germany|German state]] of [[Thuringia]]. It has an area of {{convert|269.17|km2|sqmi|1|abbr=on|lk=on}}. In December 2015 it had a population of 210,000 people. < ref > [https://www.citypopulation.de/php/germany-thuringen.php?cityid=16051000 www.citypopulation.de.Erfurt]. Retrieved 3 July 2017 < /ref >  The River [[Gera (river)|Gera]] runs through the city. 
 
 ==History== 
 People have lived in the area for about 100,000 years. The city was first mentioned in writing in 742, in a letter to the [[Pope]] from St Boniface. St Boniface established Christianity in the area. He set up a church in Erfurt in 742. Erfurt Cathedral was later built on the same site. < ref > {{cite book|last=Heinemeyer|first=Karl|title=Bonifatius: Heidenopfer, Christuskreuz, Eichenkult|year=2004|publisher=Stadtverwaltung Erfurt|pages=73–87|editor=Hardy Eidam |editor2=Marina Moritz |editor3=Gerd-Rainer Riedel |editor4=Kai-Uwe Schierz|language=German|chapter=Bonifatius in Mitteldeutschland}} < /ref > < ref > Christoph Engels, (2010) '1000 Sacred Places', Tandem Verlag GmbH, p 55 < /ref > 
 
 The city is in the centre of Germany. It was on the Via Regia, a [[medieval]] trade road network that stretched across Europe. It became an important trading centre in the [[Middle Ages]]. Erfurt was a member of the [[Hanseatic League]], an alliance of trade associations and market towns. It was also famous for producing woad. Blue indigo dye is made from the woad plant. < ref name=Raßloff > Raßloff, Steffen (2012) ''Geschichte der Stadt Erfurt''. Erfurt: Sutton Verlag. < /ref > < ref name=Stade > Stade, Heinz, et. al (2015) ''Erfurt: eine Stadt im Wandel'', Leipzig: Edition Leipzig. < /ref > 
 
 Erfurt is on the [[Way of St. James]] pilgrams' path (Spanish: ''Camino de Santigo''; German: ''Jakobsweg''). In the Middle Ages a number of [[Monastery|monasteries]] were set up in the city. [[Martin Luther]], the father of the [[Reformation]], lived at St. Augustine's Monastery from 1505 until 1511. A famous [[philosopher]] and [[theologian]] called Meister Eckhart lived at the [[Dominican Order|Dominican]] Monastery from about 1275 until 1311. < ref name=Raßloff / > 
 
 The city was part of the [[Holy Roman Empire]]. It became part of the [[Kingdom of Prussia]] in 1802. From 1949 until 1990 Erfurt was in the [[communist]] [[German Democratic Republic]] (East Germany). < ref name=Raßloff / > 
 
 Seventeen people [[Erfurt massacre|were killed]] in a [[mass shooting]] in 2002. 
 
 ==Industry== 
 Modern day Erfurt is a [[transport hub]] for high speed trains and other German and European transport networks. This makes [[logistics]] one of its main industries. Other important industries are [[agriculture]], [[horticulture]] and microelectronics. < ref name=Stade / > 
 
 The [[Bundesarbeitsgericht|German Federal Labour Court]] (German:''Bundesarbeitsgericht'') is in Erfurt. 
 
 ==Education== 
 The University of Erfurt was first set up in 1379. It closed in 1816, but was reopened in 1994. Martin Luther studied there from 1501 to 1505. < ref name=Raßloff / >  It is also thought that [[Johannes Gutenberg]], who developed the printing press, enrolled there in 1418. < ref > {{cite book|last=Martin|first=Henri-Jean|title=The History and Power of Writing|year=1995|publisher=University of Chicago Press|isbn=0-226-50836-6|page=217|chapter=The arrival of print}} < /ref > < ref > {{cite book|last=Dudley|first=Leonard|title=Information revolutions in the history of the West|url=https://archive.org/details/informationrevol00dudl|year=2008|publisher=Edward Elgar|location=Northampton, MA|isbn=978-1-84720-790-6|page=[https://archive.org/details/informationrevol00dudl/page/78 78]|chapter=The Map-maker's son}} < /ref >  Erfurt also has a ''Fachhochschule'', a University of Applied Sciences. 
 
 ==Tourist attractions== 
 Many visitors come to see the historic medieval town centre. One of the main tourist attractions is the Krämerbrücke (the Merchants' Bridge). The bridge was built in its current form in 1472. It has buildings with shops and houses on it. People still live on it. < ref name=Stade / > 
 
 The city is well known for its festivals throughout the year. Two million people visit the Christmas markets each year. < ref > [http://weihnachtsmarkt.erfurt.de/wm/en/index.html The official Erfurt Christmas Market Portal ]. Retrieved 4 July 2017 < /ref > 
 
 Other important sites in the city include: 
 * St. Augustine's Monastery, where Martin Luther lived from 1505 to 1511. It has been nominated as a [[World Heritage Site]]. < ref name=extapp > [http://www.welterbe-luther.de/en/extension-application/augustinian-monastery-erfurt UNESCO World Heritage. Augustinian Monastery, Erfurt extension application] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170804013636/http://www.welterbe-luther.de/en/extension-application/augustinian-monastery-erfurt |date=2017-08-04 }}. Retrieved 29 May 2017. < /ref > 
 * The [[Old Synagogue (Erfurt)|Old Synagogue]], which dates back to the 11th and 12th centuries. It is one of the oldest synagogues in Europe and it has been nominated as a World Heritage Site. < ref name=unesco > Permanent Delegation of Germany to UNESCO (2015) [http://whc.unesco.org/en/tentativelists/5982/ Old synagogue and Mikveh in Erfurt - UNESCO world heritage centre]. Retrieved 04 June 2017 < /ref > 
 * Erfurt Cathedral and the Severikirche (St. Severus Church), which overlook Domplatz, the Cathedral square. 
 * Petersberg [[Citadel]], a 16th-century fortress on a hill overlooking the town centre. It is one of the largest and best preserved town fortresses in Europe. < ref > [http://www.erfurt-web.de/Petersberg Petersberg - Erfurt-web.de] (in German) Retrieved 05 July 2017). < /ref >
