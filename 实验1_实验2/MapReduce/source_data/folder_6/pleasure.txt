 '''Pleasure''' is an [[emotion]]. It is the opposite of [[pain]]. [[Humans]] and many other [[mammals]] feel pleasure. < ref name= " face " > {{Cite web |url=http://www.lsa.umich.edu/psych/research%26labs/berridge/VideoIndex.htm |title=Videos of Pleasure-elicited Reactions. |access-date=2020-12-30 |archive-date=2018-01-25 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20180125013114/https://lsa.umich.edu/psych/research%26labs/berridge/VideoIndex.htm |url-status=dead }} < /ref >  People feel pleasure when they do something that is fun or that feels good.  
 
 ==Pleasure in neuroscience== 
 Pleasure is studied in [[neuroscience]]. < ref name=Brain > {{cite book |last1=Kringelbach |first1=Morten L. | last2=Berridge |first2=Kent C. |date=2010 |title=Pleasures of the Brain |publisher=Oxford University Press|isbn=9781444357929 }} < /ref >  Neuroscientists have mapped pleasure  " hotspots "  in the brain. < ref name=Brain/ >  Pleasure is important for a person's [[Health#Mental_health|mental health]] and well-being. < ref name=Brain/ >  The loss of pleasure is common in people with [[mental illness|mental illnesses]] like [[Depression (mood)|depression]], [[schizophrenia]], and [[addiction]]. < ref name=Brain/ > 
 
 ==Pleasure in psychology== 
 Pleasure is studied in positive [[psychology]]. < ref name=Lopez > {{cite book |last=Lopez |first=Shane J. |date=2011 |title=The Encyclopedia of Positive Psychology |publisher=John Wiley  &  Sons|isbn=9781444357929 }} < /ref >  How much pleasure someone feels changes from person to person. Pleasure depends how special something is. There is no rule that says what pleasure is for every person.  < ref name= Lopez/ > 
 
 [[Sigmund Freud]] wrote about the  " pleasure principle "  in the 1920 essay ''Beyond the Pleasure Principle''. < ref name= " Hothersall, D 2004. p. 290 " > Hothersall, D. 2004.  " History of Psychology " , 4th ed., Mcgraw-Hill:NY p. 290 < /ref >  According to Freud, the pleasure principle is what drives people to do things that give them a feeling of immediate [[wikt:gratification|gratification]]. < ref name= " Hothersall, D 2004. p. 290 " / > 
 
 ==Pleasure in philosophy== 
 The [[Ancient Greece|ancient Greek]] [[philosopher]] [[Epicurus]] said that people feel the most pleasure possible when they have no [[suffering]]. < ref name= " Epicurus1 " > [http://wiki.epicurus.info/Principal_Doctrines The Forty Principal Doctrines] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160212221338/http://wiki.epicurus.info/Principal_Doctrines |date=2016-02-12 }}, Number III. < /ref > 
 
 [[Hedonism]] is another [[philosophy]] that is focused on pleasure. It says that pleasure is good. < ref name= " stanford " > [http://plato.stanford.edu/entries/hedonism/ Hedonism], 2004-04-20 Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy < /ref >  In Hedonism, people focus on pleasure and avoid [[pain]].  
 
 [[Utilitarianism]] is a [[philosophy]] that focuses on pleasure. It says that [[morality]] requires that people do what has the most [[utility]] for the most people. < ref > {{cite web | url=http://www.constitution.org/jb/frag_gov.htm | title=A Fragment on Government | year=1776 | access-date=31 January 2013 | author=Bentham, Jeremy}} < /ref >
