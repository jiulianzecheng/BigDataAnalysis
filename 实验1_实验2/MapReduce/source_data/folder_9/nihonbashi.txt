 {{nihongo|'''Nihonbashi'''|日本橋|extra=literally ''Japan Bridge''}}, also romanized as '''Nihombashi''', is a business district of [[Chūō, Tokyo|Chūō]] in [[Tokyo]], [[Japan]]. The district grew up around the [[bridge]] of the same name. < ref name= " chuo " > Chuo City Tourist Association, [http://www.chuo-kanko.or.jp/english/nihonbashi/profile.html  " Nihonbashi " ] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080315052742/http://www.chuo-kanko.or.jp/english/nihonbashi/profile.html |date=2008-03-15 }}. Retrieved 2012-3-25. < /ref >  The [[bridge]] connects two sides of the Nihonbashi River.  A series of bridges at the same site have had the same name since the 17th century. < ref name= " chuo " / > 
 
 Traditionally, all distances in Japan are measured from the centerpoint of the Nihonbashi Bridge.  This custom began in the [[Edo period]]. < ref > Richie, Donald. (1994). [https://books.google.com/books?id=7T72asVTOXoC & pg=PA15 & dq= ''Introducing Tokyo,'' p. 15]. < /ref > 
 
 ==History== 
 [[File:NihombashiDatum.jpg|thumb|right|Marker at [[wikt:center|center]] of Nihonbashi]] 
 In the [[Edo period]], the Nihonbashi Bridge was the start of [[Tōkaidō (road)|Tōkaidō road]] which connected [[Edo]] and [[Kyoto]]. < ref > Nussbaum, Louis-Frédéric. (2005). [https://books.google.com/books?id=p2QnPijAEmEC & pg=PA973 & dq=  " ''Tōkaidō'' "  ] in ''Japan Encyclopedia'', p. 973. < /ref >  The earliest Nihonbashi Bridge was made of wood.  It was completed in 1603. The current bridge is made of stone.  It was completed in 1911. < ref name= " chuo " / > 
 
 In 2009, Nihonbashi was [[wikt:recognize|recognized]] as one of the [[100 Landscapes of Japan (Heisei period)|100 Landscapes of Japan]] which best show [[wikt:contemporary|contemporary]] Japan and its culture in the [[Heisei period]]. < ref > [http://www.yomiuri.co.jp/dy/features/heisei100/announcement/index.htm  " 100 Landscapes of Heisei announced, " ] ''Yomiuri Shimbun''. May 2009. Retrieved 2012-3-30. < /ref > 
 
 ===Timeline=== 
 * '''1603''' (''[[Keichō]] 8''): Wooden bridge built. < ref name= " chuo " / > 
 * '''1605''' (''Keichō 10''): The first official map of Japan was ordered with distances  measured from Nihonbashi. < ref name= " traganeou " > Traganeou, Jilly. (2004). ''The Tokaido Road: Traveling and Representation in Edo and Meiji Japan'', p. 230. < /ref >   
 * '''1911''' (''[[Meiji period|Meiji]] 44''): Bridge was re-built using stone. < ref name= " chuo " / >
