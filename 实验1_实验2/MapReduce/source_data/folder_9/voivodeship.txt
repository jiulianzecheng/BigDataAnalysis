 A '''voivodeship''', also spelled '''voivodship''', '''voivodina''' or '''vojvodina''' < ref > {{Citation|title=Translated from the Polish: The Fates, Feats, and Foibles of Polish Literature in English|date=2018-12-31|url=http://dx.doi.org/10.3138/9781442622517-018|work=Being Poland|pages=308–326|publisher=University of Toronto Press|doi=10.3138/9781442622517-018 |isbn=9781442622517 |s2cid=239373369 |access-date=2022-04-08}} < /ref >  ([[Romanian language|Romanian]]: ''voievodat'', [[Polish language|Polish]]: ''województwo'', [[Serbian language|Serbian]]: ''vojvodina'' (војводина), ''vojvodstvo'' (војводство) or ''vojvodovina'' (војводовина), [[Hungarian language|Hungarian]]: ''vajdaság'', [[Lithuanian language|Lithuanian]]: ''vaivadija'', [[Latin]] ''Palatinatus'' in Poland), is a [[geography|geographical]] [[administrative division]] dating to medieval [[Romania]], [[Hungary]], [[Poland]], [[Lithuania]], [[Latvia]], [[Russia]] and [[Serbia]] (see [[Vojvodina]]), ruled by a [[voivode|voivod]] (''voivode'', ''wojewoda''). The voivod (literal translation:  " the one who leads the warriors " , is the same as to Dux Exercituum / Herzog) was originally the military commander next to the ruler.
