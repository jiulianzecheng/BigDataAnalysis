
 '''Hakushu''' (拍手) refers to the ceremonial [[clapping]] practice in Shinto, also known as  " Kashiwade. " < ref > {{Cite web|title=Encyclopedia of Shinto詳細|url=https://d-museum.kokugakuin.ac.jp/eos/detail/id=8972|access-date=2023-03-11|website=國學院大學デジタルミュージアム|language=ja}} < /ref >  This form of clapping is quiet and gentle, and unlike western applause. < ref > {{Cite web|title=Kashiwade (Ceremonial Clapping|url=https://issuu.com/charliesan763/docs/how_to_draw_manga_vol._11_maids___miko/s/11610807|access-date=2023-02-16|website=issuu|language=en}} < /ref > 
 
 Its origin is unknown. Some people think it holds a Buddhist meaning where the right hand symbolizes Buddha, and the left hand represents all beings, thus signifying the unity of Buddha and all beings. < ref > {{Cite web|title=Why are the hands clapped when praying at shrines (Shintoism) and placed together when praying at temples (Buddhism)? Online Atlas english school. Teaching jobs in Osaka,Sapporo,Yokohama,Nagoya,Kyoto,Kobe|url=http://atlasp.net/questions/4.html|access-date=2023-02-12|website=atlasp.net}} < /ref > 
 
 Due to Shinbutsu-shūgō the origins of this practice are unclear. Shinto and Buddhism have been together so long that many things could come from either. < ref > {{Cite web|date=2013-04-14|title=Why do some Japanese people clap their hands when they pray for something at the temple?|url=https://travelience.com/faq2/why-some-japanese-people-are-clapping-hands-when-they-wish-something-at-the-temple/|access-date=2023-02-12|website=GoWithGuide by Travelience|language=en}} < /ref >  The origin must be ancient though as it is found in the [[Wajinden]] describing the [[Yayoi period|Yayoi]] rituals. < ref > https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/%E9%AD%8F%E5%BF%97%E5%80%AD%E4%BA%BA%E4%BC%9D < /ref >
