'''Coverture''' is a long-standing [[legal]] practice in the [[United States]] that comes from the [[English law|English common law]]. < ref name=AllgorNWHM > {{cite web |url=https://www.nwhm.org/blog/coverture-the-word-you-probably-don%E2%80%99t-know-but-should/ |title=Coverture — The Word You Probably Don’t Know But Should |author=Catherine Allgor |publisher=National Women's History Museum |access-date=December 4, 2016 |archive-date=November 6, 2016 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161106163955/https://www.nwhm.org/blog/coverture-the-word-you-probably-don%E2%80%99t-know-but-should/ |url-status=dead }} < /ref >  Coverture holds that a man and a woman are a single [[legal entity]]—that of the husband. < ref name=AllgorNWHM/ >  A married woman loses her own legal obligations and rights, and becomes  " covered "  by her husband. < ref name=AllgorNWHM/ >  [[wikt:traditionally|Traditionally]] a woman took her husband's [[last name]] as a [[wikt:symbol|symbol]] of this [[wikt:identity|identity]]. < ref name=AllgorNWHM/ >  A female child was covered by her father's identity. < ref name=womenlaw > {{cite web |url=http://www.library.hbs.edu/hc/wes/collections/women_law/ |title=Women and the Law |website=Women, Enterprise  &  Society |publisher=President and Fellows of Harvard College |access-date=December 4, 2016 |archive-date=May 6, 2019 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20190506132312/https://www.library.hbs.edu/hc/wes/collections/women_law/ |url-status=dead }} < /ref >  When she married that coverage transferred to her husband. < ref name=womenlaw/ >  Under this system a woman did not legally [[wikt:exist|exist]] and did not [[Ownership|own]] anything. < ref name=AllgorNWHM/ >    
 
 In the mid-19th century, with the rise of [[feminism]], coverture began to be [[wikt:criticize|criticize]]d as being unfair to women. < ref name=AmHistUSA > {{cite web |url=https://www.americanhistoryusa.com/topic/coverture/ |title=Coverture |publisher=American History USA |access-date=December 4, 2016}} < /ref >  Various laws began to be weakened and eventually done away with. < ref name=AmHistUSA/ >  But parts of coverture laws, mainly having to do with the husband being responsible for his wife's [[debt]]s, lasted into the 1960s in some parts of the U.S. < ref name=AmHistUSA/ >
